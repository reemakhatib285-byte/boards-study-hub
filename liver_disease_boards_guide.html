Section 1
Liver Structure & Function

Anatomy & Blood Supply

Basic Anatomy
  • Weight: 1–1.5 kg; 1.5–2.5% of lean body mass
  • Located in right upper quadrant, under right lower rib cage
  • Projects variably into left upper quadrant
  • Held by ligamentous attachments: diaphragm, peritoneum, great vessels, upper GI organs
Dual Blood Supply
  • Hepatic artery: ~20% — oxygen-rich blood
  • Portal vein: ~80% — nutrient-rich blood from stomach, intestines, pancreas, spleen
  • This dual supply explains why hepatic arterial occlusion alone rarely causes infarction

Histological Organization

Lobular View (Light Microscopy)
  • Portal areas at periphery
  • Central veins at center of each lobule
  • Useful for describing morphologic patterns
vs
Acinar View (Functional — Preferred)
  • Zone 1: nearest portal area — first blood contact, most oxygen-rich, most affected by toxins requiring activation
  • Zone 2: intermediate hepatocytes
  • Zone 3: nearest central vein — most susceptible to ischemia, most CYP450
  • Blood flows: Zone 1 → Zone 3
  • Bile flows in opposite direction (countercurrent): Zone 3 → Zone 1
⭐ Why the Acinar Model Matters for Boards

The acinus explains zonality of liver disease: ischemic injury → Zone 3 (pericentral) necrosis; toxic injury (acetaminophen) → Zone 3; PBC/bile acid toxicity → Zone 1. Questions about patterns of necrosis require knowing which zone is affected.

Cell Types

  • Hepatocytes: 2/3 of organ mass; main metabolic and synthetic cell
  • Kupffer cells: Resident macrophages, within sinusoidal vascular space; largest group of fixed macrophages in the body
  • Stellate (Ito) cells: In space of Disse; fat-storing; when activated → produce collagen and matrix → fibrosis
  • Sinusoidal endothelial cells: Fenestrated — allow free flow of plasma (not cells) into space of Disse
  • Space of Disse: subendothelial space where plasma contacts hepatocytes directly

Hepatocyte Polarity

Basolateral (Sinusoidal) Side
  • Lines space of Disse; richly lined with microvilli
  • Endocytotic + pinocytotic activity
  • Passive and active uptake of nutrients, proteins, molecules
Apical (Canalicular) Side
  • Forms canalicular membranes
  • Bile components are secreted here
  • Canaliculi → fuse into bile ductular elements → portal areas

Key Hepatocyte Functions

Protein Synthesis
Albumin, carrier proteins, coagulation factors, hormonal & growth factors
Bile Production
Bile acids, cholesterol, lecithin, phospholipids
Nutrient Regulation
Glucose, glycogen, lipids, cholesterol, amino acids
Metabolism & Conjugation
Bilirubin, anions, cations, drugs → excretion in bile or urine

Section 2
Patterns of Liver Disease
Hepatocellular Pattern
  • Features: liver injury, inflammation, necrosis predominate
  • ↑↑ ALT/AST, relatively less ↑ AlkP
  • Examples: viral hepatitis, alcoholic liver disease
Cholestatic (Obstructive) Pattern
  • Features: inhibition of bile flow predominates
  • ↑↑ AlkP, ↑↑ GGT, relatively less ↑ ALT
  • Examples: gallstone obstruction, PBC, drug-induced cholestasis
Mixed Pattern
  • Both hepatocellular and cholestatic features
  • Examples: cholestatic viral hepatitis, many drug-induced liver diseases
  • ↑ ALT + ↑ AlkP

Evaluation Framework: 3 Goals

1. Etiologic Diagnosis
Focus on pattern (hepatocellular vs. cholestatic), risk factors, specific testing
2. Grading (Severity)
Active vs. inactive; mild / moderate / severe disease activity
3. Staging (Course)
Early vs. late; precirrhotic vs. cirrhotic vs. end-stage

Typical Presenting Symptoms of Liver Disease

  • Fatigue — most common, most characteristic symptom; "afternoon" fatigue (after activity); intermittent, variable
  • Jaundice — hallmark; patients notice dark urine first, then scleral icterus; not detectable if bili <2.5 mg/dL
  • Itching (pruritus) — early in obstructive jaundice/drug cholestasis; later in hepatocellular; chronic in PBC/PSC (often presenting symptom before jaundice)
  • Dark urine — conjugated bilirubin (water-soluble → filtered by kidney)
  • Light stools + steatorrhea — severe cholestasis; lack of bile acids → fat malabsorption
  • Right upper quadrant pain — from stretching/irritation of Glisson's capsule; severe pain: gallbladder disease, liver abscess, sinusoidal obstruction syndrome
  • Nausea — with more severe disease; provoked by food smells or fatty foods
  • Poor appetite + weight loss — frequent in acute disease; rare in chronic unless cirrhosis present
  • Abdominal distention — ascites
  • Intestinal bleeding — portal hypertension, varices
  • Jaundice without dark urine → unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia (hemolysis, Gilbert, Crigler-Najjar)
Section 3
Clinical History

Key Risk Factors to Elicit

Exposure / Behavioral History
  • Alcohol use (quantity, duration, dependence vs. abuse)
  • Medication use: prescription, herbal compounds, OTCs, birth control pills
  • Injection drug use (IDU) — single most common risk factor for hepatitis C
  • Sexual history: # of lifetime partners; men who have sex with men (Hep B/D risk)
  • Blood transfusions:
    • Before 1986 → Hep B risk (anti-HBc screening introduced)
    • Before 1992 → Hep C risk (anti-HCV EIA introduced)
  • Travel to developing countries → Hep A, Hep E
  • Young children in day care → Hep A
  • Occupational: needlestick, blood exposure
Family History — Genetic Liver Diseases
  • Wilson disease: childhood/adolescence + family history of liver disease or neuropsychiatric disturbance
  • Hemochromatosis: cirrhosis + diabetes + endocrine failure in adulthood → check iron studies → HFE gene (C282Y, H63D)
  • α1-antitrypsin deficiency: family history of emphysema → check α1AT levels → Pi genotype
  • Familial cholestasis syndromes: PFIC, BRIC, Alagille syndrome

Hepatitis E — Special Notes

Hepatitis E — Board Pearls
  • Common cause of jaundice in Asia and Africa; uncommon in developed nations
  • In endemic areas: fecal-contaminated water transmission
  • In developed countries (autochthonous): genotypes 3 & 4, endemic in swine and wild animals (deer, wild boars)
  • Most cases in developed world: elderly men without typical risk factors for viral hepatitis
  • Chronic hepatitis E: occurs in immunosuppressed (transplant recipients, chemotherapy, HIV) → presents with abnormal enzymes without Hep B/C markers

Section 4
Physical Examination
Key Principle

The physical exam rarely uncovers liver dysfunction in asymptomatic patients with normal labs. It complements rather than replaces other diagnostics. Often normal unless disease is acute, severe, or advanced. Most signs are not specific to one diagnosis.

Typical Findings

General Liver Disease Signs

  • Icterus: best seen in sclera under natural light; mucous membranes below tongue in dark-skinned individuals; not visible if bili <2.5 mg/dL
  • Spider angiomata: superficial tortuous arterioles; fill from center outward (distinguishes from simple telangiectasias); only on arms, face, upper torso; can be pulsatile; occur in acute AND chronic disease; also in normal pregnancy
  • Palmar erythema: both acute and chronic; prominent in cirrhosis; also in normal pregnancy
  • Splenomegaly: subtle but significant → suggests underlying cirrhosis
  • Hepatomegaly: not very reliable (variability in liver size/shape); marked hepatomegaly typical of cirrhosis, sinusoidal obstruction, infiltrative disorders, metastatic cancer, alcoholic hepatitis
  • Hepatic tenderness: most reliable liver exam finding; percuss right vs. left UQ

Signs of Advanced / Decompensated Disease

  • Muscle wasting + weight loss
  • Bruising
  • Ascites (shifting dullness on percussion; confirm with US)
  • Peripheral edema — also: hypoalbuminemia, venous insufficiency, heart failure, medications
  • Dilated abdominal veins; caput medusa (collateral veins radiating from umbilicus — recanalization of umbilical vein)
  • Umbilical hernia (from ascites)
  • Hydrothorax
  • Widened pulse pressure + hyperdynamic circulation (cirrhosis: ↑ cardiac output, ↓ peripheral resistance)
  • In male patients with cirrhosis (esp. alcohol): gynecomastia, testicular atrophy, loss of male-pattern hair (hyperestrogenemia)

Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

Hepatopulmonary Syndrome — Triad
  • Liver disease + Hypoxemia + Pulmonary arteriovenous shunting
  • Platypnea: shortness of breath upright (worsens in upright position)
  • Orthodeoxia: oxygen desaturation paradoxically in upright position
  • Screening: pulse oximetry

  • Cutpoints vary among different liver diseases and populations (mostly derived from chronic Hepatitis C)

Liver Biopsy — Indications & Practicalities

When Biopsy is Indicated
  • Hepatocellular disease of uncertain cause
  • Prolonged hepatitis with possible autoimmune hepatitis
  • Unexplained hepatomegaly
  • Unexplained splenomegaly
  • Hepatic lesions uncharacterized by imaging
  • Fever of unknown origin
  • Staging malignant lymphoma
  • Critical for: autoimmune hepatitis, PBC, MASH/alcoholic steatohepatitis, Wilson disease
Technical Requirements & Contraindications
  • Adequate sample: length 1.5–2 cm with ≥10 portal tracts for accurate fibrosis assessment
  • Safe, performed with local anesthesia + US guidance
  • Contraindications:
    • Significant ascites
    • Prolonged INR
    • → Under these: use transjugular approach
  • Liver biopsy is NOT the initial procedure in cholestasis — first assess biliary tree for obstruction
  • In acute liver disease: usually NOT needed; reserved for diagnosis unclear despite workup
Section 6
Grading, Staging & Scoring Systems
Grading (Activity)
  • Severity of disease activity: active/inactive; mild/moderate/severe
  • Liver biopsy most accurate
  • ALT/AST serve as noninvasive markers but do not always reliably reflect severity
  • Examples: normal ALT in Hep B carrier state or mild chronic Hep B; normal ALT in chronic Hep C despite moderate activity; unreliable in alcoholic/MASH
  • Numerical scales: METAVIR, Histology Activity Index (HAI)
vs
Staging (Fibrosis Progression)
  • Position in natural history: early/late; precirrhotic/cirrhotic/end-stage
  • Degree of fibrosis: METAVIR scale: 0–4; Ishak scale: 0–6
  • Clinical/biochemical/imaging: only become abnormal in middle-to-late cirrhosis
  • Earliest noninvasive signs of worsening fibrosis: mild ↑ bilirubin, prolonged PT, slight ↓ albumin, mild thrombocytopenia (often first sign)
  • Importance: prognosis, therapy recommendation, complication screening

MELD Score — Current Transplant System

MELD Score — Variables & Use
  • Variables: INR (prothrombin time), serum bilirubin, serum creatinine
  • MELD 3.0 (updated): also includes serum sodium + reweighted components; incorporates albumin and sodium
  • Designed to predict short-term mortality of patients awaiting orthotopic liver transplant and after TIPS
  • Provides more objective measure than Child-Pugh; less center-to-center variation; wider range of values
  • Currently used to establish priority listing for liver transplantation in the US
  • PELD score: used for children <12 years
  • Calculator: https://optn.transplant.hrsa.gov
TE (Transient Elastography) for Staging
Useful in staging cirrhosis; predicts complications: variceal hemorrhage, ascites development, liver-related death

Cirrhosis Surveillance — What Cirrhotic Patients Need

Variceal Surveillance
  • Cirrhosis → upper endoscopy to assess for varices
  • If large varices: chronic beta-blocker therapy OR endoscopic obliteration
HCC Surveillance
  • Cirrhosis → screening + long-term surveillance for HCC
  • Appropriate approach: US liver every 6–12 months

General Management Principles for Liver Disease Patients

  • Alcohol: use sparingly or not at all; abstinence mandated for: alcohol-related liver disease, cirrhosis, interferon-based therapy for Hep B/D, antiviral therapy for Hep C
  • Medications: exercise caution with all medications; drug-induced hepatotoxicity can mimic many liver diseases and exacerbate chronic hepatitis
  • Vaccinations: all liver disease patients → Hep A + Hep B vaccine (if not vaccinated); screen for Hep C; follow CDC adult vaccination recommendations
Section 7
Evaluation of Liver Function — LFTs
Core Principle

Liver tests rarely suggest a specific diagnosis — they suggest a category (hepatocellular vs. cholestatic). Many tests (ALT, AST, AlkP) don't actually measure liver function — they detect cell damage or bile flow interference. Use them as a battery for sensitivity and specificity.

Serum Bilirubin

Normal Values
  • Total bilirubin: 0.2–0.9 mg/dL (95% of population); upper limit ~1–1.5 mg/dL
  • Conjugated (direct) upper limit: 0.3 mg/dL
  • If direct fraction <15% of total → consider ALL bilirubin as indirect
Fractionation Rule
  • Unconjugated (indirect): isolated elevation → hemolytic disorders, Crigler-Najjar, Gilbert syndrome; NOT usually liver disease
  • Conjugated (direct): elevation almost always implies liver or biliary tract disease; rate-limiting step is transport into bile canaliculi, not conjugation
  • In most liver diseases: both fractions elevated
  • Fractionation rarely helpful except for initial differentiation
Bilirubinuria Pearl

Unconjugated bilirubin is bound to albumin → NOT filtered by kidney → any bilirubin in urine = conjugated bilirubin = liver disease or obstructive jaundice. Test is ~100% accurate. False positive: phenothiazines with Ictotest. In recovering jaundice: urine bilirubin clears before serum bilirubin.

Prognostic Value of Bilirubin

  • Viral hepatitis: higher serum bilirubin → greater hepatocellular damage
  • Total serum bilirubin correlates with poor outcomes in alcoholic hepatitis
  • Critical component of MELD score
  • Elevated in drug-induced liver disease → indicates more severe injury
  • Prognostic value in PBC

Aminotransferases (ALT & AST)

AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase)
  • Found in: liver, cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle, kidneys, brain, pancreas, lungs, leukocytes, erythrocytes
  • Less specific for liver injury
vs
ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)
  • Found primarily in the liver
  • More specific indicator of liver injury
  • Low level in alcoholic liver disease: alcohol-induced deficiency of pyridoxal phosphate
AST:ALT Ratio — Diagnostic Interpretation
RatioInterpretation

⚠️ In alcohol-related liver disease: AST rarely >300 IU/L; ALT often normal

When Aminotransferases Exceed 1000 IU/L

Striking elevations (>1000 IU/L) occur almost exclusively in:

  1. Viral hepatitis
  2. Ischemic liver injury (prolonged hypotension, acute heart failure)
  3. Toxin- or drug-induced liver injury

Exception: Acute biliary obstruction from gallstone passing into common bile duct can transiently produce 1000–2000 IU/L — but decreases quickly and pattern evolves to cholestatic.

Alkaline Phosphatase (AlkP)

AlkP Key Points
  • Found in or near bile canalicular membrane of hepatocytes
  • Normal serum AlkP consists of isoenzymes from: liver, bone, placenta, small intestine
  • Elevation >4× normal: primarily cholestatic liver disorders, infiltrative liver diseases (cancer, amyloidosis), bone conditions with rapid turnover (Paget's disease)
  • Level does not distinguish intrahepatic from extrahepatic cholestasis
  • To confirm liver origin: measure GGT or 5'-nucleotidase (or fractionate by electrophoresis)
  • Isolated AlkP elevation → consider early cholestasis, hepatic infiltration by tumor/granulomata, PBC, PSC, Hodgkin's disease, diabetes, hyperthyroidism, CHF, amyloidosis
  • Normal cause: age >60 (mildly elevated); blood types O and B (after fatty meal — intestinal AlkP); children/adolescents (bone growth); late pregnancy (placental AlkP)

GGT (γ-Glutamyl Transpeptidase)

  • Located in endoplasmic reticulum and bile duct epithelial cells
  • More diffuse localization → less specific for cholestasis than AlkP or 5'-nucleotidase
  • Elevated in cholestasis but also with alcohol (some advocate for occult alcohol use detection — but poor specificity)
  • Use to confirm liver origin of elevated AlkP

Tests of Biosynthetic Function

Serum Albumin
  • Synthesized exclusively by hepatocytes
  • Half-life: 18–20 days; 4% degraded per day
  • NOT a good indicator of acute/mild dysfunction (too long half-life)
  • In hepatitis: albumin <3 g/dL → suggest chronic liver disease
  • Hypoalbuminemia in chronic disease (cirrhosis) → severe liver damage + ↓ synthesis
  • Not specific for liver disease: also in protein malnutrition, protein-losing enteropathy, nephrotic syndrome, chronic infections (IL-1, TNF inhibit synthesis)
Prothrombin Time / INR
  • Clotting factors synthesized in hepatocytes (except Factor VIII — vascular endothelial cells)
  • Half-lives: Factor VII = 6 hours; fibrinogen = 5 days
  • Best acute measure of hepatic synthetic function
  • Prolonged PT: hepatitis, cirrhosis, also Vit K deficiency (obstructive jaundice, fat malabsorption)
  • PT >5 s above control not corrected by parenteral Vit K → poor prognosis in acute viral hepatitis
  • Component of MELD 3.0 score
  • Biosynthesis of factors II, VII, IX, X depends on Vitamin K

Serum Globulins

  • γ-Globulins (immunoglobulins) ↑ in chronic liver disease (esp. cirrhosis): ↑ synthesis of antibodies against intestinal bacteria due to liver failure to clear bacterial antigens
  • Diffuse polyclonal ↑ IgG (>100% increase): autoimmune hepatitis
  • ↑ IgM: primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)
  • ↑ IgA: alcoholic liver disease

Ammonia

  • Produced from protein metabolism and intestinal bacteria (colon)
  • Liver converts to urea → renal excretion; striated muscle converts to glutamine
  • Poor correlation between blood ammonia level and presence/severity of acute encephalopathy
  • Ammonia elevated even with normal hepatic function if severe portal hypertension + portal-systemic shunting
  • Elevated arterial ammonia correlates with outcome in fulminant hepatic failure

Section 8
Bilirubin Metabolism

Overview

  • Bilirubin = end product of heme degradation
  • 70–90% derived from degradation of hemoglobin of senescent red blood cells
  • Produced in periphery → transported to liver bound tightly to albumin (insoluble in water)

The 4 Steps of Bilirubin Transport (Blood → Bile)

1
Hepatocellular Uptake: carrier-mediated kinetics via OATP1B1 and OATP1B3 transporters at basolateral membrane. Identity of actual transporter still not fully elucidated.
2
Intracellular Binding: bilirubin binds as nonsubstrate ligand to glutathione-S-transferases (formerly called ligandins) → kept in solution
3
Conjugation: bilirubin conjugated with 1 or 2 glucuronic acid moieties by UGT1A1 (bilirubin-UDP-glucuronosyltransferase) → bilirubin mono- and diglucuronides. Conjugation disrupts internal hydrogen bonding → highly water-soluble. Conjugation is obligatory for canalicular excretion.
4
Biliary Excretion: conjugated bilirubin excreted across canalicular membrane by ATP-dependent transporter MRP2 (ABCC2). Additionally: some glucuronides transported into portal circulation by MRP3 (ABCC3) → reuptake by OATP1B1/1B3. (Defective MRP2 → Dubin-Johnson; defective OATP1B1/1B3 → Rotor)

UGT1 Gene Complex — Structure (Board-Relevant)

UGT1A1 Gene Architecture
  • Large complex on chromosome 2; ≥13 substrate-specific first exons (A1, A2…); 9 expressed isoforms
  • Each exon 1 has own promoter; encodes amino-terminal ~286 AA (substrate-specific)
  • Common exons 2–5: encode 245 carboxyl-terminal AA (shared by all isoforms)
  • Mutation in first exon (e.g., A1) → affects only one isoform (e.g., UGT1A1 → bilirubin conjugation)
  • Mutation in exons 2–5 → affects ALL isoforms encoded by UGT1 complex → broader spectrum of conjugation defects
  • UGT1A1 encodes the physiologically critical bilirubin-UDP-glucuronosyltransferase

Extrahepatic Bilirubin Disposition

  • Conjugated bilirubin in gut: not reabsorbed; converted by intestinal bacteria → urobilinogen (water-soluble, colorless)
  • Urobilinogen → enterohepatic circulation; excess cleared by kidneys → urine
  • Unconjugated bilirubin: does NOT reach gut ordinarily (except neonates or severe unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia like CN-I)
  • Renal bilirubin excretion:
    • Unconjugated bilirubin: too tightly bound to albumin → NOT filtered at glomerulus → never in urine
    • Conjugated bilirubin: readily filtered → appears in urine in conjugated hyperbilirubinemia
    • Kidney acts as "overflow valve" for conjugated bilirubin → renal failure can amplify jaundice in conjugated hyperbilirubinemia

Causes of Unconjugated Hyperbilirubinemia — Increased Production

Hemolysis
  • Increased bilirubin turnover → unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia
  • Hemolysis alone: sustained bili usually <4 mg/dL (bone marrow max 8× increase in RBC production)
  • Bili >4 mg/dL + hemolysis → concomitant hepatic dysfunction
  • Prolonged hemolysis → pigment gallstones (bilirubin, not cholesterol) → cholecystitis, biliary obstruction
Ineffective Erythropoiesis
  • Fraction of bilirubin from ineffective erythropoiesis ↑ in: thalassemia major, megaloblastic anemias (folate/B12 deficiency), congenital erythropoietic porphyria, lead poisoning, dyserythropoietic anemias
  • Can reach 70% of total bilirubin production → modest unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia
Miscellaneous
Massive tissue infarctions or large hematomas → transient unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia from degradation of extravascular RBCs
Section 9
Causes of Hyperbilirubinemia

Decreased Hepatic Bilirubin Clearance — Unconjugated

Decreased Hepatic Uptake
  • Contributes to unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia of Gilbert syndrome
  • Drugs inhibiting bilirubin uptake: flavaspidic acid, novobiocin, rifampin, cholecystographic contrast agents → resolves with stopping drug
Neonatal/Physiologic Jaundice
  • Fetal bilirubin: cleared by placenta → maternal liver
  • At birth: neonatal liver assumes responsibility; UGT1A1 levels are LOW; intestinal flora undeveloped → enterohepatic circulation of unconjugated bilirubin
  • Most neonates: mild unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia, days 2–5; peak <5–10 mg/dL; resolves in 2 weeks
  • Risk of kernicterus (bilirubin encephalopathy): rapidly rising unconjugated bili or >20 mg/dL → bilirubin crosses immature BBB → deposits in basal ganglia → neurologic deficits to death
  • Treatment: phototherapy (converts bilirubin to water-soluble photoisomers excreted directly into bile); exchange transfusion
Acquired Conjugation Defects
  • Advanced hepatitis or cirrhosis: modest reduction in conjugation capacity; but conjugation better preserved than canalicular excretion
  • Drugs inhibiting UGT1A1: pregnanediol, novobiocin, chloramphenicol, gentamicin, atazanavir → unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia
  • Breast milk jaundice: fatty acids in breast milk inhibit UGT1A1; may also ↑ enterohepatic circulation
  • Transient familial neonatal hyperbilirubinemia (Lucey-Driscoll syndrome): UGT1A1 inhibitor in maternal serum

Crigler-Najjar Type I (CN-I) — Deep Dive

CN-I Clinical Features
  • Striking unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia ~340–765 μmol/L (20–45 mg/dL) — appears in neonatal period, persists for life
  • Other liver tests: normal; no hemolysis; normal hepatic histology (except occasional bile plugs)
  • Bilirubin glucuronides: virtually absent from bile
  • NO detectable UGT1A1 activity; NO response to phenobarbital
  • Prior to phototherapy: most died of kernicterus in infancy or early childhood
  • Rare: prevalence 0.6–1.0 per million; many from isolated communities (consanguinity); autosomal recessive
  • Type IA: mutations in exons 2–5 → broader conjugation defects (multiple substrates including drugs, xenobiotics)
  • Type IB: mutation in exon A1 → defect limited largely to bilirubin conjugation
  • Estrogen glucuronidation (mediated by UGT1A1) is defective in all CN-I patients

Gilbert Syndrome — Deep Dive

Gilbert Syndrome — Boards Essentials
  • Most common hereditary hyperbilirubinemia; prevalence up to 8%; males predominate (1.5:1 to >7:1)
  • Serum bilirubin: usually <3 mg/dL (fluctuates); 25% will transiently normalize
  • Exacerbated by: fasting, stress, fatigue, alcohol, intercurrent illness, reduced caloric intake
  • Improved by: increased caloric intake, enzyme-inducing agents
  • Molecular basis (Europe/US): homozygous insertion of extra TA in UGT1A1 promoter → A[TA]7TAA (vs. normal A[TA]6TAA) → ↓ UGT1A1 expression; prevalence of A[TA]7TAA allele: 30% in Western population → 9% homozygotes
  • UGT1A1 activity: 10–35% of normal
  • Hepatic bilirubin clearance: reduced to ~1/3 of normal
  • DRUG INTERACTION BOARD TRAP: CPT-11 (irinotecan) active metabolite SN-38 is glucuronidated by UGT1A1 → patients with GS are at risk for severe diarrhea and myelosuppression from irinotecan
  • HIV protease inhibitors indinavir and atazanavir inhibit UGT1A1 → hyperbilirubinemia (most pronounced in pre-existing GS)

PFIC type 3 is the ONLY type with high GGT. PFIC 1 and 2, and BRIC 1 and 2 → normal or mildly elevated GGT despite severe cholestasis. Also: mutations in FXR (NR1H4), TJP2, and USP53 cause PFIC-like disease with normal GGT.

Benign Recurrent Intrahepatic Cholestasis (BRIC)

BRIC Key Features
  • Recurrent attacks of pruritus + jaundice
  • Episode: begins with malaise + ↑ aminotransferases → then ↑ AlkP + conjugated bilirubin + jaundice + itching
  • First 1–2 episodes may be misdiagnosed as acute viral hepatitis
  • Duration: weeks to months; full clinical & biochemical resolution between attacks
  • Between episodes: physical exam normal; serum bile acids, bilirubin, transaminases, AlkP all normal
  • Autosomal recessive; does NOT lead to cirrhosis or end-stage liver disease (benign)
  • But: episodes can be prolonged and debilitating; some patients underwent liver transplantation for intractable symptoms
  • BRIC1: FIC1 mutations (same gene as PFIC1 — different mutations cause episodic vs. progressive)
  • BRIC2: BSEP mutations (same gene as PFIC2)

★ Section 12 — Board Exam Traps
Exam Traps — Internal Medicine Stage A
★ Trap 1 — Look-Alike Trap
A 28-year-old man presents with jaundice after 48 hours of fasting for Ramadan. Total bilirubin is 3.8 mg/dL with 90% indirect fraction. ALT, AST, AlkP, albumin, and PT are all normal. No bilirubinuria. Which investigation is most important next?
✗ Wrong answer: Liver biopsy to rule out hepatitis — tempting because bilirubin is elevated and the patient is young
✓ Correct: Workup for hemolysis (peripheral smear, reticulocyte count, haptoglobin, Coombs test) — and if negative, diagnose Gilbert syndrome. No further workup needed.
💡 Why: Isolated unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia (<15% direct) + normal liver tests + no bilirubinuria in an otherwise healthy young patient = Gilbert syndrome if hemolysis excluded. The fasting provocation is a classic trigger. Liver biopsy is NOT indicated.
Keyword: "fasting" + isolated unconjugated + normal liver tests
★ Trap 2 — Number Trap
A patient with acute viral hepatitis is jaundiced. When can you first detect jaundice clinically?
✗ Wrong: When total bilirubin exceeds 1.5 mg/dL (the upper limit of normal)
✓ Correct: When total bilirubin reaches ≥2.5 mg/dL (43 μmol/L). Clinically, patients typically notice dark urine BEFORE they notice scleral icterus.
💡 Why: Jaundice requires bilirubin to exceed the threshold for visible scleral coloration. Dark urine (bilirubinuria from conjugated bilirubin) appears earlier. Board questions may give a patient with dark urine but no visible jaundice and ask what the bilirubin range must be.
Keyword: 2.5 mg/dL threshold / dark urine before icterus
★ Trap 3 — Mechanism Trap
A 45-year-old woman with chronic alcoholism is found to have AST of 180 IU/L and ALT of 48 IU/L. The ALT is "almost normal." Why is ALT low in alcoholic liver disease despite significant liver injury?
✗ Wrong: Low ALT means liver disease is mild — do not pursue workup
✓ Correct: Alcohol causes a deficiency of pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6) → ↓ ALT synthesis → falsely low ALT despite liver injury. The AST:ALT ratio >2:1 (here 3.75:1) is highly suggestive of alcohol-related liver disease.
💡 Why: In alcoholic liver disease, AST rarely exceeds 300 IU/L and ALT is often normal due to the pyridoxal phosphate effect. The ratio is the key — not the absolute values. AST:ALT >2:1 is suggestive; >3:1 is highly suggestive.
Keyword: AST:ALT ratio >2 + pyridoxal phosphate deficiency
★ Trap 4 — Drug Interaction Trap
A 35-year-old man is diagnosed with metastatic colon cancer and is started on irinotecan (CPT-11) chemotherapy. Before starting treatment, genetic testing reveals he is homozygous for the A[TA]7TAA UGT1A1 promoter variant. What complication should you specifically warn him about?
✗ Wrong: This variant only causes benign jaundice (Gilbert syndrome) — no concern with chemotherapy
✓ Correct: Gilbert syndrome significantly increases the risk of severe irinotecan toxicity — specifically intractable diarrhea and myelosuppression — because UGT1A1 is responsible for glucuronidating irinotecan's active metabolite SN-38.
💡 Why: Gilbert syndrome is "benign" for bilirubin metabolism — but not for drug metabolism. SN-38 accumulates in GS patients → severe toxicity. This is the ONE clinically important exception to Gilbert syndrome being benign.
Keyword: irinotecan (CPT-11) + SN-38 + Gilbert syndrome → toxicity
★ Trap 5 — Look-Alike Trap
A 25-year-old woman develops jaundice during her first pregnancy. Total bilirubin 4.5 mg/dL, predominantly conjugated, with bilirubinuria. All other liver tests are normal. Ultrasound: no bile duct dilation, gallbladder NOT visualized. Urinary coproporphyrins: total normal, but >80% is isomer I. Which diagnosis fits?
✗ Wrong: Cholestasis of pregnancy (intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy) — tempting because jaundice is new in pregnancy
✓ Correct: Dubin-Johnson Syndrome — unmasked by pregnancy. The diagnostic clue is: normal total urinary coproporphyrins with >80% as isomer I + non-visualized gallbladder on oral cholecystography + conjugated hyperbilirubinemia with normal other tests.
💡 Why: DJS often goes undetected until pregnancy or OCP use triggers hyperbilirubinemia. The coproporphyrin pattern is pathognomonic: total normal (unlike Rotor where total is increased) but isomer I >80% (vs. normal ~75% isomer III). Rotor has increased total coproporphyrins with isomer I elevated but <70%.
Keyword: coproporphyrin isomer I >80% + normal total + non-visualized GB
★ Trap 6 — Mechanism Trap (Cholestasis vs Hepatocellular)
A 50-year-old woman with known primary biliary cholangitis has AlkP 480 IU/L (4× ULN), GGT elevated, total bilirubin 3.5 mg/dL, ALT 60 IU/L (1.5× ULN). What is the pattern, and which additional antibody test is most expected to be positive?
✗ Wrong: This is a hepatocellular pattern because bilirubin is elevated; check ANA
✓ Correct: This is a cholestatic pattern (↑↑ AlkP with proportionally minimal ↑ ALT). The expected antibody is Antimitochondrial Antibody (AMA) — positive in PBC. IgM is elevated, not IgG.
💡 Why: Pattern recognition: AlkP >4× with minimal ALT = cholestatic. PBC is one of the commonest causes of isolated or predominantly elevated AlkP. AMA is the diagnostic marker. Elevated IgM (not IgG) is the immunoglobulin pattern in PBC.
Keyword: AMA + ↑ IgM + cholestatic pattern = PBC
★ Trap 7 — Timing Trap (Liver Biopsy)
A 60-year-old man presents with 3 weeks of progressively worsening jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, and itching. AlkP is 6× ULN. The hospitalist orders a liver biopsy to evaluate the cause of cholestasis. What should be done first instead?
✗ Wrong: Proceed with liver biopsy — it is the gold standard
✓ Correct: Liver biopsy should NOT be the initial procedure in cholestasis. The biliary tree must first be assessed for obstruction with ultrasound (first-line) or MRCP/CT. Only if obstruction is excluded and cause remains unclear should biopsy be considered.
💡 Why: Cholestatic pattern with pale stools = possible extrahepatic biliary obstruction (tumor, stone, stricture). Proceeding directly to biopsy risks missing a surgically correctable cause and can be complicated by dilated ducts. Ultrasound first; MRCP/ERCP as needed.
Keyword: cholestatic → image first → biopsy only if obstruction excluded
★ Trap 8 — Prognosis Trap (Acute Liver Failure)
A 24-year-old woman presents with acute hepatitis A infection. On day 5, she becomes confused and is found to have asterixis and disorientation. INR is 3.0. What is the significance of this finding?
✗ Wrong: This is expected hepatic encephalopathy in hepatitis — manage conservatively
✓ Correct: The appearance of hepatic encephalopathy during acute hepatitis is the major criterion for diagnosis of ACUTE LIVER FAILURE, which carries a poor prognosis. Urgent evaluation for liver transplantation is required.
💡 Why: Hepatic encephalopathy is not a "complication" of acute hepatitis — it redefines the diagnosis to Acute Liver Failure (ALF). ALF in Hep A is rare but occurs. The MELD score + PT uncorrectable by Vitamin K = poor prognostic indicators. Transplantation evaluation should begin immediately.
Keyword: encephalopathy + acute hepatitis = acute liver failure → poor prognosis
★ Trap 9 — Population Trap (Hepatitis E)
A 70-year-old kidney transplant recipient on tacrolimus and mycophenolate presents with 8 weeks of elevated aminotransferases and bilirubin. Hepatitis B and C serologies are negative. What diagnosis should be considered that is often missed in this population?
✗ Wrong: Drug-induced liver injury from immunosuppressives — most common assumption
✓ Correct: Chronic Hepatitis E (HEV infection). In immunosuppressed patients (transplant, chemotherapy, HIV), HEV can cause chronic hepatitis presenting as persistently elevated enzymes with negative Hep B/C markers. Check anti-HEV IgM and HEV RNA.
💡 Why: HEV typically causes acute self-limited disease but can become CHRONIC in immunosuppressed patients. This is a board favorite — it won't present as the classic "traveler with jaundice" but as an elderly transplant recipient with unexplained chronic liver test abnormalities. HEV testing is specifically indicated here.
Keyword: immunosuppressed + chronic elevated enzymes + negative B/C → check HEV
★ Trap 10 — Number Trap (PFIC)
A 6-month-old infant presents with severe progressive cholestasis, jaundice, pruritus, and growth retardation. Serum bilirubin and AlkP are markedly elevated. GGT is surprisingly NORMAL. What type of PFIC does this suggest?
✗ Wrong: PFIC type 3 — "PFIC has high GGT"
✓ Correct: PFIC type 1 (FIC1 mutation) or PFIC type 2 (BSEP mutation) — both present with severe cholestasis and NORMAL or LOW GGT. Only PFIC type 3 (MDR3 mutation) has HIGH GGT.
💡 Why: The GGT pattern is the critical distinguishing feature. In PFIC 1 & 2, GGT is normal despite severe cholestasis. This is counterintuitive and is therefore a classic board trap. MDR3 (PFIC3) is the only type with elevated GGT because the phospholipid transport defect causes different bile composition affecting GGT expression.
Keyword: severe cholestasis + normal GGT in infant = PFIC 1 or 2 (NOT PFIC 3)
