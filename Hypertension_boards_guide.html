BP Classification & Treatment Triggers

Classification is by the higher of SBP or DBP. Requires average of ≥2 readings at ≥2 visits. SBP is overestimated by ~7 mmHg in practice.

ACC/AHA Classification

CategorySBPDBPManagementRecheck
Normal<120<80Encourage healthy lifestyle12 months
Elevated120–129<80Active nonpharmacologic therapy3–6 months
Stage 1 HTN130–13980–89Lifestyle ± drug if high CVD risk1–3 months
Stage 2 HTN≥140≥90Lifestyle + drug immediately1 month
🎯 Treatment Target

SBP/DBP <130/80 mmHg — universally, for most patients regardless of comorbidities (including LVH, DM, CKD).

Pregnancy exception: target <140/90 (more intensive not proven superior in RCTs).

💡 Stage 1 — When to Start Drugs
  • Low CVD risk → lifestyle first, reassess 3–6 months
  • Established CVD or high ASCVD risk → lifestyle + drug immediately
  • At 130–139 mmHg, CVD risk varies >30-fold depending on other risk factors

White Coat vs Masked Hypertension

White Coat HTN
  • Office BP meets HTN criteria
  • Out-of-office BPs normal
  • Prevalence: 15–25%
  • CVD risk: lower — closer to normotensives
  • Management: nonpharmacologic + close monitoring
Masked HTN
  • Office BP normal
  • Out-of-office BPs meet HTN criteria
  • Prevalence: 15–25%
  • CVD risk: high — similar to sustained HTN
  • Suspect: elevated-normal office BP + LVH or proteinuria
  • Management: antihypertensive drugs + nonpharm
⚠ Board Traps — BP Targets & Stage 1

Trap 1: "Target SBP <120 with LVH." → Wrong. Target is <130 mmHg universally — not lower. <120 was the SPRINT intensive arm but is not the guideline target.

Trap 2: "Treat all Stage 1 HTN with drugs immediately." → Wrong. Low-risk Stage 1 → lifestyle first, reassess 3–6 months. Only high-risk Stage 1 starts drugs immediately.

Board keyword: Stage 1 low-risk = lifestyle first; Stage 2 = drug always; target = <130/80 universally

Beta Blockers — Generations & Receptors

Not first-line for HTN without a compelling indication. Know the generations, receptors, and key board traps around propranolol and 3rd-gen agents.

Three Generations

GenerationDrugsReceptorsKey Feature / Board Point
1stPropranolol, Nadolol, Timololβ1 + β2 (non-selective)Bronchospasm risk; masks hypoglycaemia; avoid in asthma/COPD
2ndMetoprolol, Atenolol, Bisoprololβ1 (cardioselective)Less bronchospasm. Atenolol: inferior cardioprotection vs diuretics — NOT preferred for HTN
3rdCarvedilol, Labetalol, Nebivololβ1+β2+α1 (carvedilol/labetalol) or β1+NO (nebivolol)Additional vasodilation. Labetalol → pregnancy. Carvedilol → HFrEF
Propranolol — The Trap Drug
  • Non-selective β1 + β2
  • NO ISA (that's pindolol)
  • Contraindicated: asthma/COPD
  • Masks hypoglycaemia in DM
Carvedilol (3rd Gen)
  • β1 + β2 + α1 blocker
  • Used in HFrEF
  • NOT purely beta-selective
Labetalol (3rd Gen)
  • β1 + β2 + α1 blocker
  • Drug of choice in hypertensive emergency in pregnancy
  • IV formulation available
⚠ Board Trap — Propranolol Receptors

Trap 1: "Propranolol is a selective β1 blocker." → Wrong. Non-selective β1 + β2.

Trap 2: "Propranolol has sympathomimetic activity." → Wrong. No ISA. Pindolol has ISA.

Trap 3: "Carvedilol is purely beta-selective." → Wrong. Also blocks α1 → vasodilation.

Board keyword: Propranolol = non-selective β1+β2; no ISA; Carvedilol = β+α1
03

Alpha Blockers & MRAs

Alpha Blockers

Doxazosin / Prazosin / Terazosin
  • Block α1 → peripheral vasodilation
  • Best use: men with BPH + HTN (dual benefit)
  • NOT first-line for HTN — less cardioprotective than diuretics
  • ALLHAT doxazosin arm stopped early: excess HF events vs chlorthalidone
  • Best for stroke prevention: amlodipine, not doxazosin
⚠ Phaeochromocytoma — NOT Doxazosin

Phaeochromocytoma requires phenoxybenzamine — a non-selective, irreversible alpha blocker.

Doxazosin is selective + reversible → insufficient for pheo's catecholamine surges. Using it alone risks dangerous hypertensive crisis during manipulation.

MRAs — Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists

Three MRAs
DrugSelectivityKey Feature
SpironolactoneNon-selectiveGynecomastia, ED in men (anti-androgenic)
EplerenoneSelectiveNo anti-androgenic SE → use when spiro not tolerated
FinerenoneNon-steroidal, selectiveFDA 2023; better K⁺ safety; for CKD + DM
Best Indications
  • Resistant HTN — 4th drug to add (most important board indication)
  • Primary aldosteronism (bilateral hyperplasia)
  • Low-renin states — aldosterone is the driver
  • HFrEF (spiro/eplerenone)
  • CKD + DM (finerenone)
⚠ Side Effects — Critical

All MRAs: Hyperkalemia (retain K⁺) — NOT hypokalemia. Monitor K⁺ carefully, especially in CKD.

Spironolactone only: gynecomastia, breast tenderness, erectile dysfunction → switch to eplerenone or finerenone if intolerable.

⚠ Board Traps — MRAs & Pheo

Trap 1: "MRAs cause hypokalemia." → Wrong. They block aldosterone → retain K⁺ → hyperkalemia.

Trap 2: "All MRAs cause gynaecomastia." → Wrong. Only spironolactone (anti-androgenic). Eplerenone and finerenone are selective — no sex hormone SE.

Trap 3: "MRAs are best in high-renin HTN." → Wrong. They work in low-renin states (aldosterone-driven).

Trap 4: "Use doxazosin for phaeochromocytoma." → Wrong. Use phenoxybenzamine (non-selective, irreversible).

Board keyword: MRA = hyperkalemia, not hypokalemia; MRA = low-renin states; Pheo = phenoxybenzamine
04

Thiazides — Side Effects & K⁺ Management

Preferred Thiazide: Chlorthalidone > HCTZ

Chlorthalidone (Preferred)
  • Half-life ~45–60h → better 24h nocturnal BP control
  • Used in virtually all landmark US trials: ALLHAT, SPRINT
  • Effective even in advanced CKD (stage 4)
  • Doses: 12.5–25 mg once daily
HCTZ (Inferior)
  • Half-life ~6–12h → wears off overnight; misses morning BP surge
  • Not used in major cardiovascular outcome trials
  • Still widely used but pharmacokinetically inferior

Metabolic Side Effects — All Six

Electrolytes
  • Hypokalemia ← most tested
  • Hyponatremia
  • Hypercalcemia ← board trap (not hypocalcemia)
Metabolic
  • Hyperuricemia → gout risk
  • Hyperglycemia (mild)
  • Dyslipidaemia (transient)
Benefit
  • ↓ Urinary calcium excretion → ↑ osteoblast activity → ↓ fracture risk in postmenopausal women (boards may test hypercalcemia as a benefit)

Managing Thiazide-Induced Hypokalemia — Decision Tree

Patient on thiazide → K⁺ low
↓
Option 1: Add K⁺-sparing diuretic (Amiloride or Triamterene) ← Board Answer Q5
↓ or
Option 2: Add ACEi or ARB (also retain K⁺)
↓ or
Option 3: Add oral potassium supplement (Slow-K) ← Q12: K=3, BP still high
↓ NEVER
Add furosemide → loop diuretic = MORE K⁺ loss → worsens hypokalemia
⚠ Board Traps — Furosemide & Calcium

Trap 1: "Add furosemide to manage thiazide-induced hypokalemia." → Wrong. Furosemide is a loop diuretic — it causes more potassium wasting. Correct answer: amiloride/triamterene or oral K⁺ supplement.

Trap 2: "CCBs correct thiazide-induced hypokalemia." → Wrong. CCBs lower BP but have no effect on potassium levels.

Trap 3: "Thiazides cause hypocalcemia." → Wrong. Thiazides cause hypercalcemia — they retain calcium in the distal tubule.

Board keyword: Thiazide + low K⁺ → amiloride/triamterene; NEVER furosemide; thiazides = HYPERcalcemia
05

First-Line Strategy & Key Trials

First-Line Classes — Four Options

First-Line (Any of These)

Thiazide diuretic (chlorthalidone) · CCB (amlodipine) · ACEi · ARB

Beta blockers NOT first-line unless compelling indication: IHD, HFrEF, AF rate control, arrhythmia. Consistently inferior for stroke in head-to-head RCTs (especially atenolol).

Race-Based Starting Strategy

General Population (Non-Black)
  • Monotherapy: any first-line class
  • Dual: Diuretic + ACEi/ARB or CCB + ACEi/ARB
  • Triple: Diuretic + CCB + ACEi/ARB
Black Patients — Different First Step
  • ACEi/ARB less effective as monotherapy (lower baseline renin activity)
  • Start with: Diuretic + CCB — preferred initial combination
  • Combination therapy usually required from the start
  • K⁺ supplementation: SBP ↓ ~3× greater in Black vs White adults

Key Trials — Board-Critical

ALLHAT (Board-Critical)

Compared: Chlorthalidone vs Amlodipine vs Lisinopril vs Doxazosin

  • All three equally reduced CV events and mortality
  • CCB (amlodipine) superior for stroke prevention
  • Diuretic (chlorthalidone) superior for HF prevention
  • Doxazosin arm stopped early — excess HF events
ACCOMPLISH Trial

Compared: ACEi + CCB (benazepril + amlodipine) vs ACEi + thiazide

  • ACEi + CCB superior in reducing CV events
  • But ALLHAT showed ACEi + thiazide not worse overall → both combinations acceptable
⚠ Board Traps — ALLHAT & Combination Therapy

Trap 1: "ALLHAT showed diuretics are superior to everything." → Wrong. All three were equal for CV mortality. Differences only in specific endpoints (stroke → CCB; HF → diuretic).

Trap 2: "ACCOMPLISH showed thiazide superior to CCB when combined with ACEi." → Wrong. CCB + ACEi was better.

Trap 3: "Combining ACEi + ARB is more effective and renoprotective." → Wrong and dangerous. No additional CV benefit; significantly increases hyperkalemia and AKI risk in CKD. Do NOT combine.

Board keyword: ALLHAT = equal CV mortality; stroke → CCB; HF → diuretic; ACEi+ARB = dangerous combination
06

Drug Ladder — Resistant HTN

Resistant HTN: BP ≥130/80 on 3 drugs including a diuretic, OR needing 4+ drugs to reach target. First exclude pseudoresistance (white coat, non-adherence, wrong cuff, short-acting diuretic).

Step-Up Strategy

Monotherapy — Stage 1, low risk, near target. Any first-line: chlorthalidone, amlodipine, ACEi, or ARB.
Dual therapy — most patients. Diuretic + ACEi/ARB or CCB + ACEi/ARB. Preferred pairs: ACEI/ARB + diuretic or ACEI/ARB + CCB (ACCOMPLISH).
Triple therapy — Diuretic + CCB + ACEi/ARB. Single-pill combination strongly preferred for adherence.
Resistant HTN — Add MRA (Spironolactone) as 4th drug. If not tolerated (gynaecomastia/ED) → switch to eplerenone or finerenone. Before adding 4th drug: switch HCTZ → chlorthalidone first.
💜 Why MRA Works in Resistant HTN

Most patients with resistant HTN have low-renin, aldosterone-driven volume expansion that is under-treated by standard drug combinations. Spironolactone blocks aldosterone at the distal tubule — often the missing piece. This is why it is the specific 4th-drug recommendation.

⚠ Before Adding a 4th Drug — Check These First
  • Rule out pseudoresistance: white coat effect (ambulatory monitoring), medication non-adherence, wrong cuff size
  • Switch HCTZ → chlorthalidone (longer half-life = better 24h control)
  • Screen for secondary causes (primary aldosteronism, OSA, renal artery stenosis)
  • Eliminate contributing medications (NSAIDs, OCPs, decongestants, steroids)

Hypertensive Emergency vs Urgency

The Critical Distinction

Urgency
  • BP ≥180/100–110
  • No end-organ damage
  • Treatment: oral agents, outpatient
  • No specific BP target; lower gradually
  • No requirement for IV drugs
Emergency
  • BP ≥180/110
  • + Active end-organ damage
  • Treatment: IV drugs, ER/inpatient
  • Target: reduce by ~25% in first hour — do NOT normalise immediately
  • Exception: aortic dissection → SBP <120 urgently

What Counts as End-Organ Damage

✅ YES — Hypertensive Emergency
  • Hypertensive encephalopathy (headache, vision changes, seizures, confusion)
  • Acute LV failure / pulmonary oedema
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Aortic dissection
  • Eclampsia / severe pre-eclampsia
  • Phaeochromocytoma crisis
  • Acute coronary syndrome
❌ NOT an Emergency Sign
  • Small kidneys → chronic CKD; not an acute finding
  • RBBB on ECG → not acutely caused by HTN
  • Chronically abnormal labs
  • Papilledema alone without full encephalopathy

IV Drugs for Hypertensive Emergency

DrugClassPreferred When
NicardipineDHP CCB (IV)Most common; smooth, titratable; preferred generally
Labetalolα+β blocker (IV)Pregnancy; aortic dissection; most emergencies
Sodium nitroprussideDirect vasodilator (IV)Severe emergencies; cyanide toxicity with prolonged use
HydralazineDirect vasodilator (IV)Pregnancy; less titratable
PhentolamineAlpha blocker (IV)Phaeochromocytoma crisis; cocaine-induced HTN
⚠ Board Traps — Emergency Management

Trap 1: "Small kidneys = sign of hypertensive emergency." → Wrong. Small kidneys = chronic CKD — not an acute finding.

Trap 2: "Normalise BP immediately in hypertensive emergency." → Dangerous. Rule: reduce by ~25% in the first hour. Rapid normalisation → ischaemic stroke, AKI, or coronary events from too-rapid pressure reduction.

Trap 3: "CVA with hypertension → lower BP immediately." → Wrong. Most stroke patients should NOT have aggressive acute BP lowering — impaired autoregulation; rapid reduction worsens ischaemic injury.

Trap 4: "Aortic dissection — standard 25% reduction target." → Wrong. Dissection needs SBP <120 urgently — the most aggressive target of any HTN emergency.

Board keyword: Small kidneys = chronic CKD (not emergency); 25% in 1h; dissection = <120 SBP; no rapid normalisation

Ambulatory BP Monitoring

Ambulatory BP monitoring provides the best CVD risk prediction — superior to office BP alone. It detects white coat HTN, masked HTN, and non-dipping patterns.

Equivalent BP Values

MeasurementEquivalent to Office 130/80
Office BP130/80 mmHg
Daytime ambulatory130/80 mmHg
Nighttime ambulatory110/65 mmHg
24h average125/75 mmHg
🏠 White Coat HTN

High in office + normal outside. Ambulatory monitoring reveals true BP. Avoids unnecessary treatment.

🎭 Masked HTN

Normal in office + high outside. CVD risk high (similar to sustained HTN). Ambulatory monitoring avoids under-treatment.

🌙 Non-Dippers

Normal nocturnal dip = 10–20% fall. Non-dippers: no nocturnal fall → worse CVD prognosis. More common in non-Hispanic Black patients.

⚠ Board Trap — Non-Dippers

Trap: "Patients without a nocturnal dip have better prognosis." → Wrong. Non-dippers have worse prognosis — failure to dip maintains sustained cardiovascular stress, independent risk factor for target organ damage.

Also wrong: "Ambulatory monitoring is only useful to detect white coat HTN." → It also detects masked HTN, non-dipping patterns, and provides better CVD risk prediction than office BP alone.

Board keyword: Non-dipper = WORSE prognosis, not better

Secondary HTN & Special Scenarios

When to Work Up for Secondary Causes

Suspect Secondary HTN If:
  • Treatment-resistant HTN on 3+ drugs including a diuretic
  • Abrupt worsening of previously controlled HTN
  • Disproportionate end-organ damage for BP level
  • Unprovoked hypokalemia, proteinuria, or LVH
  • Onset before age 30 or very severe HTN

Pulse Pressure — Wide vs Narrow

Wide Pulse Pressure (↑SBP − DBP)

Stiff vessels or high stroke volume:

  • Peripheral artery disease (board answer Q3)
  • Aortic regurgitation
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Severe anaemia
  • Old age (aortic stiffening)
Narrow Pulse Pressure (↓SBP − DBP)

Fixed obstruction or low output:

  • Aortic stenosis ← board trap (NOT wide PP)
  • HOCM
  • Severe HF / tamponade
  • Hypothyroidism
⚠ Board Traps — Secondary HTN

Trap 1: "Aortic stenosis causes wide pulse pressure." → Wrong. AS = narrow PP (fixed obstruction → low SV).

Trap 2: "Use doxazosin for phaeochromocytoma." → Wrong. Use phenoxybenzamine — irreversible, non-selective. Giving beta-blocker before alpha-blocker in pheo → unopposed alpha → fatal hypertensive crisis.

Trap 3: "CPAP cures HTN in OSA." → Wrong. CPAP has minimal BP-lowering effect. Treat HTN pharmacologically regardless of CPAP.

Board keyword: AS = narrow PP; pheo = alpha FIRST then beta; CPAP = minimal BP benefit

HTN in Special Populations

CKD + HTN

🫘 Preferred Management
  • ACEi or ARB — reduce intraglomerular pressure, protect against proteinuria
  • Diuretics important; loop diuretics if eGFR <30 (OR chlorthalidone — now shown effective even in stage 4 CKD)
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: BP ↓~5/2 mmHg + substantial renoprotection
  • Do NOT combine ACEi + ARB → dangerous: ↑hyperkalemia + accelerated GFR decline
⚠ Bilateral RAS — ACEI Danger

Bilateral renal artery stenosis: both kidneys depend on angiotensin II–driven efferent tone to maintain GFR. ACEI removes this → acute kidney injury + hyperkalemia → stop the ACEI immediately.

Pregnancy + HTN

✅ Safe Drugs in Pregnancy
  • Labetalol — first-line; good safety record; may be superior for preeclampsia prevention
  • Nifedipine (long-acting CCB)
  • Methyldopa — long history of safety
  • Hydralazine IV — for acute severe HTN
  • Target: <140/90 mmHg (not <130/80)
🚫 Absolutely Contraindicated in Pregnancy
  • ACEi — teratogenic: fetal renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios, neonatal anuria
  • ARBs — same mechanism of fetal RAAS suppression
  • Atenolol — associated with fetal growth restriction

Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy

DisorderDefinitionKey Feature
Gestational HTNNew HTN ≥20 weeks, no proteinuriaOften resolves postpartum
PreeclampsiaHTN + proteinuria OR end-organ damage ≥20 weeks3–5% of pregnancies; ↑long-term CV risk for mother
HELLP SyndromeHemolysis + Elevated LFTs + Low PlateletsMedical emergency; may require early delivery
EclampsiaPreeclampsia + seizuresIV magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis
⚠ Board Traps — Special Populations

Trap 1: "ACEi is the preferred drug in pregnancy." → Absolutely wrong. ACEi and ARB are teratogenic — fetal renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios.

Trap 2: "Stop enalapril in CKD with proteinuria to protect kidneys." → Wrong. ACEi is specifically indicated — intensify management, do not stop.

Trap 3: "ACEi safe in bilateral RAS." → Wrong. Both kidneys depend on efferent angiotensin II tone; ACEI → AKI → stop immediately.

Board keyword: Pregnancy = ACEi/ARB absolutely contraindicated; CKD + proteinuria = ACEi/ARB essential; bilateral RAS = stop ACEi

⚠ Board Trap — Smoking Cessation & BP
Q: Which lifestyle intervention does NOT directly lower blood pressure?

Trap: "Smoking cessation is a proven BP-lowering intervention."

Why wrong: Smoking cessation is critical for overall CV risk reduction — but it is not proven to directly lower blood pressure. On board questions asking which intervention does NOT lower BP, smoking cessation is the answer.

Also wrong: "Exercise must be intense to lower BP." Moderate aerobic exercise ≥150 min/week is sufficient for a ~5–7 mmHg reduction.

Board keyword: Smoking cessation = CV benefit YES, BP lowering NO

Clonidine & Rebound Hypertension

Clonidine — Mechanism & Role
  • Central α2-agonist → reduces sympathetic outflow → lowers BP
  • Side effects: sedation, dry mouth, bradycardia, constipation
  • NOT first-line for HTN — used when other drugs fail or in resistant HTN
⚡ Unique Danger — Rebound HTN

Abrupt discontinuation → sympathetic surge → rebound hypertension — can be severe and life-threatening.

Only clonidine causes this among common antihypertensives. Stopping ACEi, ARB, doxazosin, or carvedilol does NOT produce this rebound.

⚠ Board Trap — Clonidine Rebound
Scenario: Patient on multiple drugs including clonidine suddenly stops all medications. Which drug causes the most dangerous rebound?

Trap: "Stopping ACEi / doxazosin / carvedilol → rebound HTN."

Why wrong: Only clonidine causes clinically significant rebound HTN. The mechanism: sympathetic rebound surge after withdrawal of central α2 inhibition. Other antihypertensives do not produce this phenomenon.

Correct: Clonidine → rebound HTN crisis.

Board keyword: Abrupt clonidine discontinuation = sympathetic surge = rebound HTN crisis; not ACEi, not doxazosin

Exam Traps — Board Exam Coach

All tested traps from the question bank, written as full board-coach vignettes. Each includes: vignette, distractor, correct answer, mechanism, and board keyword.

⚠ Trap 01 — ACEI Cough: Switch to ARB, Not Another ACEI
52F, HTN + CKD + proteinuria, on lisinopril 6 months. BP 124/76. Persistent dry cough started 2 weeks after initiation. Next step?

Trap: "Switch to a different ACEI (e.g., enalapril)."

Why wrong: All ACEIs block bradykinin degradation — cough is a class effect. Switching within the class doesn't resolve it.

Correct: Switch to an ARB (losartan or valsartan). ARBs block AT₁ receptors without touching bradykinin → no cough, same renoprotection in CKD.

Board keyword: ACEI cough = bradykinin = class effect → switch to ARB
⚠ Trap 02 — ACEi + ARB Combination: Dangerous, Not Additive
60M, HTN + DM + CKD (eGFR 42, proteinuria 800 mg/day), on lisinopril 40 mg. BP 148/90. What to add?

Trap: "Add an ARB for double RAAS blockade — superior renoprotection."

Why wrong: ACEi + ARB → ↑hyperkalemia + ↑AKI in CKD. Meta-analyses show no additional CV or renal benefit over monotherapy. The combination is contraindicated.

Correct: Add amlodipine (CCB) or chlorthalidone (thiazide). Complementary mechanisms for additive BP lowering.

Board keyword: ACEi + ARB = dangerous; no extra benefit; add CCB or diuretic instead
⚠ Trap 03 — Phaeochromocytoma: Alpha Before Beta
38F, episodic severe headaches + sweating + palpitations. BP 210/120 during episode. Urine metanephrines elevated. CT: 3cm right adrenal mass. Pre-op management?

Trap: "Start a beta-blocker first to control HR and BP."

Why wrong: Beta-blocker alone blocks β₂ vasodilation without blocking α₁ vasoconstriction → unopposed alpha → severe, potentially fatal hypertensive crisis during surgical manipulation.

Correct: Phenoxybenzamine (alpha-blocker) FIRST, then add beta-blocker if tachycardia persists.

Board keyword: Pheo = alpha FIRST, then beta; beta first alone = fatal
⚠ Trap 04 — Primary Aldosteronism: Gold-Standard Test
48M, resistant HTN on 3 drugs, K⁺ 2.9 mEq/L (unprovoked — no diuretics, no diarrhea). Gold-standard diagnostic test?

Trap: "CT abdomen — to find the adenoma."

Why wrong: CT misses up to 30% of adenomas and cannot reliably distinguish unilateral (adenoma → surgery) from bilateral (hyperplasia → MRA). CT is a screening adjunct, not gold standard.

Correct: Adrenal vein sampling. Screening: plasma aldosterone/renin ratio first.

Board keyword: Primary aldosteronism gold standard = adrenal vein sampling, NOT CT
⚠ Trap 05 — Pregnancy HTN: Absolutely Contraindicated Drugs
29F, 16 weeks pregnant, BP 158/98. No prior HTN. Which antihypertensive is CONTRAINDICATED?

Trap: Students select atenolol (avoid due to fetal growth restriction) or hydralazine (actually safe).

Why wrong about atenolol: Atenolol is avoided (fetal growth restriction) but not absolutely contraindicated.

Correct: ACEis and ARBs. Both are category D/X in pregnancy — fetal renal dysgenesis, skull hypoplasia, oligohydramnios, neonatal anuria (RAAS suppression during nephrogenesis). Safe alternatives: labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa.

Board keyword: ACEi/ARB in pregnancy = fetal renal toxicity; absolutely contraindicated
⚠ Trap 06 — Non-DHP CCB + Beta-Blocker: Dangerous Combination
65M, HTN + new AF (HR 110), started on diltiazem for rate control. BP 155/92 remains. Which antihypertensive NOT to add?

Trap: "Add a beta-blocker to maximise rate control."

Why wrong: Both non-DHP CCBs (diltiazem/verapamil) and beta-blockers independently slow AV node conduction. Combining → severe bradycardia, high-degree AV block, or asystole.

Correct: Add chlorthalidone or ACEi/ARB for BP control. Do NOT add a beta-blocker to diltiazem/verapamil.

Board keyword: Diltiazem/verapamil + beta-blocker = AV block / asystole
⚠ Trap 07 — Fibromuscular Dysplasia: Young Woman, Abrupt HTN
32F, White, no prior medical history, BMI 21, no family HTN, BP 175/110 over 2 weeks. Abdominal bruit. Most likely diagnosis and best treatment?

Trap: "Atherosclerotic renovascular HTN — medical management."

Why wrong: Atherosclerotic RVD occurs in older adults with ASCVD risk factors. This young, slender woman with abrupt-onset HTN and an abdominal bruit = FMD.

Correct: Fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD). Treatment: percutaneous transluminal angioplasty (PTA) — often curative. Confirm with CTA/MRA/DSA (gold standard). "String-of-beads" appearance on angiography.

Board keyword: Young White woman + abrupt HTN + bruit = FMD → PTA often curative
⚠ Trap 08 — Resistant HTN: Switch HCTZ → Chlorthalidone First
58M, resistant HTN on HCTZ 25 mg + lisinopril 40 mg + amlodipine 10 mg. BP 155/96, adherent. No secondary causes. What before adding a 4th drug?

Trap: "Add spironolactone as the 4th agent now."

Why wrong: HCTZ has a short half-life (~6–12h), missing nocturnal BP control. Switching to chlorthalidone (t½ ~45–60h) alone can significantly reduce BP before adding a 4th drug.

Correct sequence: (1) Ensure adherence → (2) Switch HCTZ to chlorthalidone → (3) Consider single-pill combination → (4) THEN add MRA if still uncontrolled.

Board keyword: Resistant HTN on HCTZ = switch to chlorthalidone BEFORE adding 4th drug
⚠ Trap 09 — Bilateral RAS + ACEI = Acute Kidney Injury
72M, bilateral RAS, CKD (eGFR 38), started lisinopril. 1 week later: creatinine 1.8 → 3.1 mg/dL, K⁺ 5.8 mEq/L. Mechanism and action?

Trap: "Continue ACEI — renoprotective in CKD."

Why wrong: In bilateral RAS, both kidneys depend on angiotensin II–driven efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction to maintain GFR. ACEI removes this support → ↓glomerular filtration pressure → AKI. ACEI is renoprotective in unilateral RAS and CKD with proteinuria — but in bilateral RAS it causes AKI.

Correct: Stop the ACEI immediately.

Board keyword: Bilateral RAS + ACEI = AKI (both kidneys lose efferent tone) → stop immediately
⚠ Trap 10 — Spironolactone SE: Switch to Eplerenone, Don't Stop MRA
55M, resistant HTN, well-controlled on spironolactone 50 mg. BP 126/78. Now reports bilateral breast tenderness and erectile dysfunction. K⁺ 4.2. Next step?

Trap: "Discontinue the MRA — the drug class is causing intolerable side effects."

Why wrong: The drug class is still the right choice (4th agent for resistant HTN, excellent control). The side effects are anti-androgenic — specific to spironolactone. Eplerenone and finerenone are more mineralocorticoid-selective and do not cause these SE.

Correct: Switch spironolactone to eplerenone or finerenone. Same BP efficacy, no sex hormone side effects.

Board keyword: Spiro gynecomastia/ED → switch to eplerenone/finerenone; do NOT discontinue MRA class
⚠ Trap 11 — Smoking Cessation: CV Benefit Without BP Lowering
Q: Which lifestyle intervention does NOT directly lower blood pressure?

Trap: "Smoking cessation — it reduces cardiovascular risk by lowering BP."

Why wrong: Smoking cessation critically reduces overall CV risk (AMI, stroke, death) — but is not proven to directly lower blood pressure. BP reduction requires DASH diet, sodium reduction, weight loss, exercise, alcohol reduction, or potassium supplementation.

Correct: Smoking cessation. CV benefit: yes. BP lowering: no.

Board keyword: Smoking cessation = CV benefit but NOT BP lowering
