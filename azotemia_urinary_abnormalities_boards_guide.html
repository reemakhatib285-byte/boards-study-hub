Assessment of GFR — The Foundation of Renal Evaluation

Why GFR is the Primary Metric

GFR is the gold standard measure of kidney "function" — it reflects how many milliliters of plasma the kidneys completely clear of a given substance per minute. Every other lab value (creatinine, urea, cystatin C) is merely a surrogate. Understanding what each surrogate actually measures — and its limitations — is essential for boards.

  • Direct GFR measurement: Requires IV administration of a radioactive isotope (inulin or iothalamate) that is freely filtered at the glomerulus but neither reabsorbed nor secreted by the tubule. GFR = clearance (mL/min) calculated from urinary appearance rate over several hours. Not feasible in clinical practice.
  • Why GFR matters clinically: Failure to account for GFR reduction in drug dosing causes significant morbidity and death — especially digoxin and imipenem. Drug dosing is a classic board trap.

Plasma Creatinine (PCr) — The Clinical Surrogate

PCr is the most widely used GFR marker. GFR is related inversely to PCr — as GFR falls, PCr rises. However, this relationship has critical limitations that boards love to exploit.

  • Source of creatinine: Derived from muscle metabolism of creatine — generated at a relatively constant rate day to day (unlike urea, which varies with protein intake).
  • Inverse relationship: GFR falls in roughly inverse proportion to rising PCr — so doubling of PCr ≈ halving of GFR (in steady state only).
  • Problem 1 — Dietary effect: PCr rises acutely after ingestion of cooked meat (dietary creatine is converted to creatinine during cooking).
  • Problem 2 — Tubular secretion: Creatinine is secreted into the proximal tubule via an organic cation pathway, especially in advanced CKD — this overestimates true GFR.
  • Problem 3 — Muscle mass confounders: Chronic illness, glucocorticoid use, malnutrition → loss of muscle mass → PCr stays low even as GFR declines significantly. A bedridden elderly patient may have a "normal" creatinine with severely reduced GFR.
  • Chronic disease monitoring: In progressive CKD, plot 1/PCr (y-axis) vs. time (x-axis) — the slope is linear and relatively constant per individual. Deviation from the slope signals a superimposed acute process (volume depletion, drug reaction).
⚠ Board Trap — Stable PCr ≠ Stable GFR
Symptomatic uremia generally does not develop until GFR <15 mL/min. Significant GFR loss can occur with minimal or imperceptible PCr changes if muscle mass is decreasing simultaneously (e.g., elderly frail patient, chronic steroid use).

Creatinine Clearance (CrCl) — The 24-Hour Urine Method

CrCl approximates GFR using the clearance formula applied to a timed (usually 24-h) urine collection:

📐 Formula
CrCl (mL/min) = (Uvol × UCr) / (PCr × Tmin)
Where Uvol = urine volume, UCr = urine creatinine concentration, PCr = plasma creatinine, Tmin = collection time in minutes.

Adequacy of collection — verified by expected creatinine excretion per kg body weight:

Males (20–50 years)

18.5–25.0 mg/kg body weight per 24 h
Example: 80 kg man → 1,500–2,000 mg/day expected

Females (20–50 years)

16.5–22.4 mg/kg body weight per 24 h
(Less muscle mass → less creatinine production)

🚨 Limitation — Not Applicable in Unstable GFR
Every creatinine-based GFR estimation (CrCl, MDRD, CKD-EPI) assumes steady state — no daily increases or decreases in PCr. In rapidly changing AKI, these formulas are unreliable and should not be used for drug dosing decisions.

GFR Estimation Equations — Cockcroft-Gault, MDRD, and CKD-EPI

When 24-h urine is not available, two equations estimate GFR from PCr alone. Know which is more accurate at which range:

Formula

💜 Race Modifier Controversy — High Yield
The CKD-EPI coefficient of 1.159 for self-reported Black race reflects that measured GFR was 16% higher in Black vs. non-Black individuals with similar age, sex, and creatinine in the development dataset. Race is a social, not biological, construct. Many medical centers have stopped reporting race-modified eGFRs. The key consequence: removing the modifier → lower reported eGFR in Black patients → improved access to transplant waitlisting at an earlier CKD stage. Potential risks: overdiagnosis of CKD, altered drug dosing (metformin), reduced imaging access.

Cystatin C — The Alternative GFR Marker

  • Source: Produced at a constant rate from all nucleated cells — not affected by muscle mass (unlike creatinine).
  • Advantage: More sensitive marker for early GFR decline; less influenced by race and muscle mass.
  • Disadvantage: Levels influenced by sex, diabetes mellitus, smoking, and inflammation — limiting its universal applicability.
  • Current best practice: Combined creatinine + cystatin C eGFR equations are more accurate than either alone and are increasingly recommended, especially when creatinine-based estimates are unreliable (e.g., bodybuilder with elevated creatinine from muscle mass).
✅ Clinical Pearl — Bodybuilder Trap
A bodybuilder may have elevated PCr from increased muscle mass, leading to underestimation of GFR by creatinine-based equations. In this case, cystatin C eGFR is more accurate.
02

Approach to the Patient with Azotemia

Azotemia = retention of nitrogenous waste products (urea, creatinine) due to reduced GFR. It results from:

Prerenal

Reduced renal perfusion — 40–80% of AKI cases. Tubules intact. Rapidly reversible if treated.

Intrinsic Renal

Structural damage to glomeruli, tubules, interstitium, or vessels. Most commonly ATN (~90% of intrinsic AKI).

Postrenal

Obstruction of urine flow — ureters, bladder outlet, urethra. <5% of AKI. Must rule out early — reversible.

Acute vs. Chronic Renal Failure — Key Distinguishing Features

Once elevated creatinine is identified, first determine: is this acute or chronic? The history and labs often clarify this, but these specific findings help:

Acute Renal Failure
  • Recent documented decline in GFR
  • Normal-sized kidneys on ultrasound
  • Intact renal parenchyma on ultrasound
  • Anemia, hypocalcemia, hyperphosphatemia may also be present (do not exclude AKI)
Chronic Renal Failure
  • Azotemia >3 months
  • Small kidneys bilaterally — increased echogenicity, cortical thinning on US
  • Isosthenuria (non-concentrated urine, isosmotic with plasma)
  • Broad casts in urine sediment
  • Renal osteodystrophy (very late — ESRD on dialysis)
  • Proteinuria, polyuria, nocturia, edema, hypertension
  • Hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, hypocalcemia, hyperphosphatemia, hyperparathyroidism
⚠ Critical Point — Labs Don't Separate Acute from Chronic
Anemia, hypocalcemia, and hyperphosphatemia are characteristic of CKD but are also frequently present in AKI. Do not use these labs alone to distinguish acute from chronic. Use history, renal ultrasound, and urinalysis instead.

Prerenal Failure — The Most Common Cause of AKI

Decreased renal perfusion accounts for 40–80% of acute renal failure. Tubules are structurally intact — the kidney responds appropriately to perceived hypovolemia by conserving sodium and water. If appropriately treated, it is rapidly reversible.

Etiologies of Prerenal Azotemia

Decreased Circulating Volume
  • GI hemorrhage
  • Burns
  • Diarrhea
  • Diuretics
Volume Sequestration
  • Pancreatitis
  • Peritonitis
  • Rhabdomyolysis
Reduced Effective Arterial Volume
  • Cardiogenic shock
  • Sepsis (peripheral vasodilation)
  • Severe heart failure (hepatorenal)
  • NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors/ARBs

Pathophysiology — The Autoregulatory Response

True or "effective" arterial hypovolemia → fall in mean arterial pressure → triggers a cascade:

  1. Sympathetic nervous system activation — renal vasoconstriction
  2. Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone activation — angiotensin II mediates efferent arteriolar constriction (maintains glomerular filtration pressure)
  3. AVP (vasopressin) release — promotes tubular water reabsorption → concentrated urine
  4. Prostaglandin-mediated afferent arteriolar dilation — preserves GFR by increasing glomerular blood flow
🔑 Critical Threshold
Once mean arterial pressure falls below 80 mmHg, GFR declines steeply — autoregulation fails below this point.

GFR in the prerenal state is thus maintained by a balance between two autoregulatory mechanisms:

Afferent Dilation (PGE₂)
Prostaglandins dilate afferent arterioles → increase glomerular blood flow

NSAIDs block this → severe vasoconstriction → AKI risk
Efferent Constriction (AngII)
Angiotensin II constricts efferent arterioles → increases glomerular capillary filtration pressure

ACE inhibitors/ARBs block this → decreased filtration pressure → AKI risk
🚨 High-Yield Drug Interaction — NSAIDs + ACEi/ARBs
Patients taking NSAIDs and/or ACE inhibitors/ARBs are most susceptible to hemodynamically mediated AKI when blood volume or arterial perfusion pressure is reduced. In this scenario, GFR is entirely dependent on afferent PG-mediated vasodilation AND efferent Ang-II-mediated vasoconstriction simultaneously — blocking either arm precipitates AKI.
🚨 Bilateral Renal Artery Stenosis Trap
Patients with bilateral renal artery stenosis (or stenosis in a solitary kidney) are dependent on efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction for maintaining glomerular filtration pressure. ACE inhibitors or ARBs → precipitous GFR decline. This is a classic board scenario.
⚠ Prerenal → ATN Continuum
Prolonged renal hypoperfusion may lead to acute tubular necrosis (ATN) — structural tubular injury superimposed on prerenal state. ATN occurs when hypoperfusion persists long enough to cause ischemic damage.

Urinary Indices — Distinguishing Prerenal from ATN

These indices reflect the functional state of the tubule. In prerenal azotemia, tubules are intact and respond normally. In ATN, tubular function is lost.

📐 FENa Formula
FENa = (UNa × PCr) / (PNa × UCr) × 100
FENa <1% = avid Na retention = tubules intact = prerenal
FENa >2% = Na wasting = tubular dysfunction = ATN
⚠ FENa Exceptions — When <1% Doesn't Mean Prerenal
FENa may be <1% even in ATN in these situations:
  • Milder, nonoliguric ATN — e.g., rhabdomyolysis — tubular function partially preserved
  • Underlying "prerenal" disorders — CHF, cirrhosis, hepatorenal syndrome — avid Na retention persists even with tubular injury
  • Contrast nephropathy — early
💜 Pearl — Urine Microscopy is King
Muddy brown casts and/or tubular epithelial cells in the urine = highly predictive of ATN. Microscopic examination of urine sediment is a key diagnostic test in AKI — more specific than any single laboratory value. Prerenal sediment = normal or hyaline/granular casts only.

Postrenal Azotemia — Always Rule Out First

Urinary tract obstruction accounts for <5% of acute renal failure, but is usually reversible and must be ruled out early in the evaluation. Even though it is uncommon, the reversibility makes early identification critical.

When Does Obstruction Cause Bilateral Renal Failure?

Since a single kidney can maintain adequate clearance, complete obstructive AKI requires one of the following:

  • Obstruction at the urethra or bladder outlet — most common in men (BPH, prostate cancer)
  • Bilateral ureteral obstruction — pelvic tumor, retroperitoneal fibrosis, bilateral calculi
  • Unilateral obstruction in a patient with a single functioning kidney

Diagnosis

  • First-line: Renal ultrasound — looks for ureteral and renal pelvic dilation (hydronephrosis)
  • Ultrasound may be negative even with obstruction when:
    • Very early in the course of obstruction
    • Ureters cannot dilate — encased by pelvic/periureteral tumors or retroperitoneal fibrosis
  • Alternative: Furosemide renogram (MAG3 nuclear medicine study) — better defines presence or absence of obstructive uropathy when ultrasound is negative but clinical suspicion remains high
⚠ Board Trap — Normal Ultrasound Does Not Exclude Obstruction
In retroperitoneal fibrosis or tumor encasement, ureters may be obstructed without dilation. A normal ultrasound does not rule out obstruction — proceed to MAG3 renogram if clinical suspicion persists.
05

Intrinsic Renal Disease — When Prerenal and Postrenal Are Excluded

When prerenal and postrenal etiologies are excluded, intrinsic parenchymal renal disease is present. The clinical setting and urinalysis are critical for differentiating among the subtypes.

Categories of Intrinsic Renal Disease

Ischemic ATN (~90% of intrinsic AKI)

Most frequent in: major surgery, trauma, severe hypovolemia, overwhelming sepsis, extensive burns.

Part of the prerenal → ATN continuum: prerenal that persists long enough causes structural tubular injury.

Nephrotoxic ATN (~90% of intrinsic AKI with ischemic)

Complicates many common medications via: intrarenal vasoconstriction, direct tubule toxicity, and/or tubular obstruction.

The kidney is vulnerable due to: rich blood supply (25% of cardiac output) and concentration/metabolism of toxins in tubular fluid.

🔑 Ischemic ATN — Clinical Setting
Ischemic + nephrotoxic ATN = ~90% of intrinsic renal failure.
Always search for: hypotension episodes + nephrotoxin exposure. Discontinuation of nephrotoxins + stabilization of blood pressure often suffice without dialysis — ongoing tubular cell regeneration occurs.

Tubulointerstitial Processes — Acute Kidney Injury Subtype

Processes involving the tubules and interstitium:

  • Drug-induced interstitial nephritis — especially antibiotics, NSAIDs, proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)
  • Severe infections — bacterial and viral
  • Systemic diseases — SLE, sarcoidosis, Sjögren's syndrome, lymphoma, leukemia

Urinalysis in Interstitial Nephritis

  • Mild to moderate proteinuria
  • Hematuria
  • Pyuria (~75% of cases)
  • Occasionally WBC casts
⚠ Board Trap — Eosinophiluria Is No Longer Recommended
The classic teaching was that >10% urinary eosinophils (Wright's or Hansel's stain) = allergic interstitial nephritis. However, urine eosinophils can be increased in several other causes of AKI (no diagnostic utility). This test is no longer recommended in the workup of AKI.
⚠ RBC Casts in Interstitial Nephritis → Think Glomerular Disease
The finding of RBC casts has been reported in interstitial nephritis but should always prompt a search for glomerular diseases. RBC casts = glomerulonephritis until proven otherwise.

Large Renal Vessel Occlusion

  • Uncommon cause of AKI. Significant GFR reduction suggests bilateral processes or unilateral in a single-kidney patient.
  • Renal arteries can be occluded by: atheroemboli, thromboemboli, in situ thrombosis, aortic dissection, vasculitis
  • Atheroembolic AKI: Often associated with recent aortic instrumentation. Cholesterol-rich emboli lodge in medium/small renal arteries → eosinophil-rich inflammatory reaction. May occur spontaneously.
💜 Atheroembolic AKI — Classic Triad
Normal urinalysis + livedo reticularis + distal peripheral infarcts + eosinophilia after aortic instrumentation → atheroembolic AKI. Biopsy confirmatory but often unnecessary when this triad is present.
Renal Artery Thrombosis
  • Mild proteinuria + hematuria
  • Confirm with angiogram
Renal Vein Thrombosis
  • Heavy proteinuria + hematuria
  • Occurs in context of nephrotic syndrome
  • Confirm with angiogram

Glomerular and Microvascular Disease — Acute Nephritic Syndrome

Diseases of glomeruli (glomerulonephritis, vasculitis) and renal microvasculature (HUS, TTP, malignant hypertension) present with glomerular injury:

  • Proteinuria
  • Hematuria
  • Reduced GFR
  • Altered sodium excretion → hypertension, edema, circulatory congestion
🚨 RBC Casts = Indication for Renal Biopsy
The finding of RBC casts in the urine is an indication for early renal biopsy — the pathologic pattern has important implications for diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment. Even in the absence of azotemia, serologic evaluation and biopsy should be done.
🔑 Hematuria Without RBC Casts
RBC casts are highly specific but insensitive for glomerulonephritis. Hematuria without RBC casts can also indicate glomerular disease. To enhance specificity: examine urine with phase-contrast microscopy for dysmorphic RBCs ("acanthocytes") — associated with glomerular disease.

Oliguria, Anuria, and Urine Volume Abnormalities

Oliguria

24-h urine output <400 mL

Oliguria is never normal — at least 400 mL of maximally concentrated urine must be produced to excrete the obligate daily osmolar load.

Anuria

24-h urine output <100 mL

Complete absence of urine formation.

Nonoliguria

Urine output >400 mL/d in a patient with acute or chronic azotemia.

Nonoliguric ATN: less severe K⁺ and H⁺ disturbances; faster recovery to normal renal function.

Causes of Anuria

  • Complete bilateral urinary tract obstruction
  • Vascular catastrophe — aortic dissection or arterial occlusion
  • Renal vein thrombosis
  • Acute cast nephropathy in myeloma
  • Renal cortical necrosis
  • Severe ATN
  • Severe rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis
  • Combined therapy with NSAIDs + ACE inhibitors/ARBs
  • Hypovolemic, cardiogenic, or septic shock
💜 Pearl — Triple Whammy Drug Combination
NSAIDs + ACE inhibitors/ARBs + diuretics = "triple whammy" = very high risk of AKI and even anuria, especially in volume-depleted patients. Classic board question setup.

Proteinuria — Mechanisms, Types, and Clinical Significance

Normal Protein Excretion

  • Total urinary protein: <150 mg/day
  • Albumin: <30 mg/day
  • Even at albuminuria <30 mg/day, risk for progression to overt nephropathy and cardiovascular disease is increased
  • Normal urine protein includes: Tamm-Horsfall protein (secreted by tubules), IgA, urokinase, small amounts of β₂-microglobulin, apoproteins, enzymes, peptide hormones

The Glomerular Filtration Barrier — Structure and Function

  1. Glomerular endothelial cells — form pores of ~100 nm; retain blood cells but offer little impediment to most proteins
  2. Glomerular basement membrane (GBM) — traps most large proteins (>100 kDa)
  3. Podocyte foot processes — cover the urinary side of GBM; produce narrow slit diaphragm channels — allow small solutes and water, but NOT proteins

Both charge selectivity (albumin is negatively charged; GBM also negatively charged → repulsion) and size selectivity normally prevent virtually all plasma albumin, globulins, and high-molecular-weight proteins from crossing.

Mechanisms of Proteinuria

Glomerular Proteinuria

Disruption of the filtration barrier — charge or size selectivity lost. Leads to albumin and/or other plasma protein leakage.

Selective (minimal change disease)

Fusion of foot processes → loss predominantly of albumin only (charge selectivity lost, size selectivity relatively preserved).

Nonselective (immune complex disease)

Disruption of both GBM and slit diaphragms → loss of albumin AND other plasma proteins. Foot process fusion also increases pressure across capillary basement membrane → larger pores → more severe proteinuria.

Tubular Proteinuria

Small proteins (<20 kDa) are freely filtered but normally reabsorbed by proximal tubule. Tubular damage impairs reabsorption → low-level proteinuria (<1 g/24 h) of small proteins.

Examples: β₂-microglobulin, lysozyme in CMML

Overflow Proteinuria

Excessive production of an abnormal protein that exceeds the reabsorptive capacity of the proximal tubule.

  • Plasma cell dyscrasias: Multiple myeloma, amyloidosis, lymphomas → monoclonal immunoglobulin light chain production → filtered by glomerulus → overwhelms proximal tubule reabsorption → light chains in urine (Bence-Jones proteins)
  • NOT detected by dipstick — dipstick detects albumin only, not light chains. Must use sulfosalicylic acid or urine protein electrophoresis (UPEP).
  • Renal failure mechanisms in myeloma: proximal tubule injury, cast nephropathy, amyloid deposition, light chain deposition — dictated by the structural characteristics of the specific monoclonal light chain. Not all excreted light chains are nephrotoxic.
  • CMML + lysozyme nephropathy: Overproduction of lysozyme → excessive proximal tubule reabsorption → severe tubulopathy with intracytoplasmic membrane-bound vacuoles on EM

🔑 Dipstick Limitations — Critical
Dipstick detects albumin only.
False positives: pH >7.0, very concentrated urine, urine contaminated with blood.
False negatives: very dilute urine (concentration effect), non-albumin proteinuria (light chains in myeloma).
For all-protein quantification: sulfosalicylic acid or trichloroacetic acid precipitation. For albumin: spot albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR) from first morning void.
🔑 Spot Protein:Creatinine Ratio
A protein/creatinine ratio of 3.0 in a random spot urine ≈ 3.0 g of proteinuria per day. Quick bedside estimate without 24-h collection.

Nephrotic Syndrome — Mechanism and Consequences

When total daily urinary protein >3.5 g, the nephrotic syndrome usually includes:

Hypoalbuminemia

Excessive urinary losses + increased proximal tubule catabolism of filtered albumin

Edema

Renal Na retention + reduced plasma oncotic pressure → fluid movement to interstitium

Hyperlipidemia

Increased hepatic lipoprotein synthesis (may be severe); compensatory hepatic production

Hypercoagulability

Urinary loss of antithrombin III, reduced proteins S and C, hyperfibrinogenemia, enhanced platelet aggregation → risk of venous thrombosis, especially renal vein thrombosis

💜 Edema Mechanism in Nephrotic Syndrome — Two Pathways
Pathway 1 (Classic): ↓ oncotic pressure → fluid leaks to interstitium → "underfilling" → activates RAAS + SNS + AVP → Na/water retention → more edema
Pathway 2 (Modern): Filtered proteases from plasma (normally retained by the filtration barrier) directly activate ENaC (epithelial Na channels) in collecting duct principal cells → primary renal Na retention, independent of oncotic pressure.
⚠ Hypertension in Nephrotic Syndrome
Hypertension is uncommon in primary kidney diseases causing nephrotic syndrome — unlike nephritic syndrome where hypertension is characteristic. This is a classic distinguishing feature.
🔑 Increased Infection Risk in Nephrotic Syndrome
Loss of immunoglobulins in urine → increased infection risk (especially encapsulated organisms). This is why nephrotic patients are particularly susceptible to Streptococcus pneumoniae.

Transient (Non-Pathologic) Causes of Proteinuria

These do not indicate kidney disease and resolve with removal of trigger:

Orthostatic / Postural proteinuria Exercise Fever Congestive heart failure Intermittent proteinuria

Hematuria, Pyuria, and Urinary Casts

Hematuria — Definition and Basic Approach

  • Definition: ≥2–5 RBCs per high-power field (HPF) on urine microscopy
  • Detected by: dipstick (detects myoglobin too — false positive in rhabdomyolysis)
  • Isolated hematuria without proteinuria, other cells, or casts → suggests bleeding from the urinary tract (not a glomerular process)

Causes of Isolated (Non-Glomerular) Hematuria

Stones (nephrolithiasis) Neoplasms Tuberculosis Trauma Prostatitis
⚠ Gross Hematuria with Blood Clots
Gross hematuria with blood clots is usually NOT an intrinsic renal process — suggests a postrenal source in the urinary collecting system. Blood clots require large-vessel bleeding; glomerular hematuria does not produce clots.

Clinical Significance by Pattern

  • Single urinalysis with hematuria: Common; may result from menstruation, viral illness, allergy, exercise, mild trauma. Usually benign.
  • Persistent/significant hematuria — defined as ANY of:
    • >3 RBCs/HPF on three separate urinalyses, OR
    • A single UA with >100 RBCs/HPF, OR
    • Gross hematuria
    → Associated with significant renal or urologic lesions in 9.1% of cases.
  • Isolated painless hematuria + nondysmorphic RBCs + increasing age → raises suspicion for urogenital neoplasm
  • Hematuria + pyuria + bacteriuria → infection — treat with antibiotics after cultures
  • Hypercalciuria and hyperuricosuria — also risk factors for unexplained hematuria in children and adults. In 50–60%, dietary intervention to reduce calcium and uric acid excretion eliminates microscopic hematuria.

Glomerular Hematuria — Distinctive Features

  • Dysmorphic RBCs — irregular shapes from pH and osmolarity changes along the distal nephron. Best detected by phase-contrast microscopy ("acanthocytes").
  • Hematuria + dysmorphic RBCs + RBC casts + protein >500 mg/day = virtually diagnostic of glomerulonephritis

Most Common Causes of Isolated Glomerular Hematuria

IgA Nephropathy

Most common glomerular hematuria cause. Episodic gross hematuria (often with upper respiratory infections). Need biopsy for definitive diagnosis.

Hereditary Nephritis (Alport)

Family history of renal failure. Episodic gross hematuria. X-linked (COL4A5 mutation) or autosomal. Need biopsy.

Thin Basement Membrane Disease

Family members with microscopic hematuria. "Benign familial hematuria." Usually good prognosis.

🚨 RBC Casts — Diagnostic and Action Threshold
RBC casts = RBCs that enter the tubule fluid and become trapped in a cylindrical mold of gelled Tamm-Horsfall protein.
RBC casts = highly specific for glomerulonephritis → early renal biopsy indicated — even in the absence of azotemia. The pathologic pattern determines diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment. Do not wait for azotemia to develop.

Pyuria and WBC Casts

  • Isolated pyuria is unusual — inflammatory reactions usually cause both hematuria and pyuria together
  • Bacteria + pyuria = infection
  • WBC casts + bacteria = pyelonephritis
  • Sterile pyuria (negative bacterial cultures) → think urogenital tuberculosis
  • WBCs and/or WBC casts also seen in: acute glomerulonephritis, tubulointerstitial processes (interstitial nephritis, transplant rejection)

Waxy and Broad Casts — Chronic Renal Disease

  • Waxy casts: Degenerated cellular casts — seen in chronic renal diseases
  • Broad casts: Arise in dilated tubules that have undergone compensatory hypertrophy in response to reduced renal mass — indicates advanced CKD with significant nephron loss

Polyuria — Differential Diagnosis and Diagnostic Approach

Polyuria = urine output >3 L/day. Patients often cannot distinguish urinary frequency (small volumes) from true polyuria — a 24-h urine collection is often needed for quantification.

Two Mechanisms of Polyuria

Solute Diuresis

Excretion of non-absorbable solutes suppresses proximal NaCl and water reabsorption.

Diagnostic: Urine osmolality >300 mosmol/L

  • Glucose — poorly controlled DM with glucosuria (most common)
  • Mannitol — iatrogenic
  • Radiocontrast media
  • High-protein feeding — enteral/parenteral → increased urea production
  • Resolving ATN or post-obstructive diuresis — tubule damage → impaired Na reabsorption + reduced aldosterone responsiveness → significant natriuresis and polyuria
  • Cystic renal diseases, Bartter syndrome
⚠ DM Solute Diuresis Trap
In hyperglycemic solute diuresis: urine Na concentration < blood Na → more water than sodium is lost → hypernatremia and hypertonicity. The patient becomes hyperosmolar.
Water Diuresis

Defect in AVP production or renal responsiveness to AVP.

Diagnostic: Urine osmolality <250 mosmol/L with urine output >3 L/day

  • Primary polydipsia — habit, psychiatric disorders, neurologic lesions, medications (thioridazine, chlorpromazine, anticholinergics)
  • Central DI — AVP production defect
  • Nephrogenic DI — renal tubule unresponsive to AVP

Primary Polydipsia — Mechanism

  • Extracellular fluid volume is normal or expanded
  • Plasma AVP levels are reduced (serum osmolality tends toward the lower limits of normal)
  • Urine osmolality maximally dilute at 50 mosmol/L
  • Low serum sodium is a clue (contrasts with DI where Na is often high or normal-high)

Central Diabetes Insipidus (DI)

Defective AVP secretion from the posterior pituitary. Causes:

  • Idiopathic — selective destruction of AVP-secreting neurons in supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei; can be autosomal dominant or sporadic
  • Secondary: Posthypophysectomy, trauma, neoplastic, inflammatory, vascular, or infectious hypothalamic disease

Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus

Renal tubule fails to respond to AVP. Acquired causes:

  • Acquired tubular diseases: pyelonephritis, analgesic nephropathy, multiple myeloma, amyloidosis, obstruction, sarcoidosis
  • Metabolic: hypercalcemia, hypokalemia
  • Autoimmune: Sjögren's syndrome, sickle cell anemia
  • Drugs/toxins: lithium (classic), demeclocycline, methoxyflurane, ethanol, diphenylhydantoin, propoxyphene, amphotericin
  • Congenital: hereditary, polycystic or medullary cystic disease
💜 Lithium — Classic Nephrogenic DI
Lithium is the most common drug cause of nephrogenic DI. It inserts into collecting duct principal cells via ENaC and inhibits the aquaporin-2 (AQP2) response to AVP. Boards love this association.

🔑 Best Diagnostic Method
Plasma AVP level is the best method for distinguishing central from nephrogenic DI.
Alternatively: assays for copeptin (cleaved from pre-pro-AVP during axonal transport in the posterior pituitary) — increasingly available in many centers.
Measurement of hypertonic saline-stimulated plasma copeptin can substitute for the water deprivation test if available.
Classical approach: water deprivation test + exogenous DDAVP — distinguishes primary polydipsia from central and nephrogenic DI.

High-Yield Clinical Distinctions

Nephrotic vs Nephritic Hypertension

Nephrotic syndrome: Hypertension is uncommon in primary kidney diseases causing nephrotic syndrome.

Nephritic syndrome: Hypertension, edema, and circulatory congestion are characteristic features.

Dipstick vs Sulfosalicylic Acid

Dipstick → detects albumin only.

Sulfosalicylic acid / trichloroacetic acid → detects all proteins including Bence-Jones (light chains).

In suspected myeloma: use UPEP (urine protein electrophoresis), not dipstick.

FENa Pitfalls

FENa <1% with ATN occurs in: rhabdomyolysis, CHF, cirrhosis, hepatorenal syndrome, contrast nephropathy.

FENa is not reliable in non-oliguric ATN.

Solute vs Water Diuresis Cutoff

Urine osmolality >300 mosmol/L with >3 L/day = solute diuresis → find the solute.

Urine osmolality <250 mosmol/L with >3 L/day = water diuresis → DI or polydipsia.

Exam Traps — Stage A Internal Medicine Board Coach

Each trap below shows exactly how the question will be framed, what the wrong answer looks like and why it's tempting, and the mechanism behind the correct answer.

Trap 01 The "Normal" Creatinine in an Elderly Woman
An 84-year-old frail woman is admitted with confusion. Her serum creatinine is 0.8 mg/dL. She weighs 42 kg and has been bedridden for 6 months. Her eGFR based on creatinine alone is calculated as 65 mL/min/1.73m². What does this actually tell you?
Correct Answer: Creatinine-based eGFR significantly overestimates true GFR in this patient.
A "normal" creatinine in a frail, low-muscle-mass elderly woman does NOT indicate normal kidney function. Creatinine is derived from muscle metabolism — severe sarcopenia from chronic illness + bedrest reduces daily creatinine production, so PCr stays low even as GFR is markedly reduced. True GFR may be <30 mL/min. Cystatin C eGFR or a 24-h urine collection would give a more accurate picture.
⚠ Trap: Most students accept PCr 0.8 as "normal kidney function." The key board keyword is sarcopenia / low muscle mass — which always signals creatinine underestimates renal impairment. Drug dosing adjustments are critical in this population.
Trap 02 NSAIDs + ACEi in a Volume-Depleted Patient
A 72-year-old man with hypertension, CKD (baseline creatinine 1.4), and osteoarthritis is on lisinopril 10 mg/day and ibuprofen 400 mg TID for joint pain. He develops gastroenteritis with 3 days of vomiting and diarrhea. He presents with creatinine 3.8 mg/dL, oliguria, and no hematuria or casts. His urine Na is 14 mEq/L and urine osmolality is 520 mosmol/L. What is the most likely cause and best next step?
Correct Answer: Hemodynamically mediated AKI from NSAIDs + ACEi in the setting of volume depletion — stop both drugs and volume resuscitate.
The urine indices (UNa <20, Uosm >500, FENa <1%) indicate the tubules are intact and avid Na conservation is ongoing — this is either prerenal or hemodynamically mediated AKI mimicking prerenal. NSAIDs blocked prostaglandin-mediated afferent dilation; ACEi blocked angiotensin II-mediated efferent constriction — both autoregulatory mechanisms are gone simultaneously, causing GFR to collapse with any degree of volume loss.
⚠ Trap: Students may diagnose "prerenal azotemia" and simply give fluids without stopping the offending drugs — the AKI will not resolve (and may worsen) if the pharmacologic insult is maintained. The board keyword is dual blockade + volume depletion.
Trap 03 Bilateral Renal Artery Stenosis + ACE Inhibitor
A 65-year-old woman with known peripheral artery disease and longstanding hypertension is started on ramipril for blood pressure control. One week later, her creatinine has risen from 1.0 to 2.4 mg/dL. She has no edema, no hematuria, and normal urinalysis. What is the most likely mechanism of her acute kidney injury?
Correct Answer: Bilateral renal artery stenosis — ACE inhibitor reduced efferent arteriolar tone, precipitating a fall in glomerular filtration pressure.
In bilateral RAS, glomerular filtration pressure is maintained by angiotensin II–mediated efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction — the only remaining compensatory mechanism given reduced afferent perfusion. ACE inhibitors/ARBs eliminate this, causing the GFR to plummet. The blank urinalysis with no hematuria or casts points away from glomerulonephritis.
⚠ Trap: Students may identify the ACEi as the culprit but think it should be continued because "the creatinine will plateau." The board keyword is bilateral RAS / PAD / peripheral vascular disease + ACEi = acute ischemic nephropathy. Stop the drug immediately.
Trap 04 FENa <1% in ATN — Rhabdomyolysis Trap
A 28-year-old man is brought in after a seizure with prolonged postictal state. His CK is 48,000 IU/L, creatinine 3.2 mg/dL, and urine is dark ("tea-colored"). Urine dipstick is positive for blood with no RBCs seen on microscopy. His FENa = 0.6%. What type of AKI is this?
Correct Answer: Acute tubular necrosis from rhabdomyolysis — despite FENa <1%.
Rhabdomyolysis causes myoglobinuria — myoglobin tests positive on dipstick for "blood" but no RBCs are seen on microscopy (false positive for hematuria). The ATN from rhabdomyolysis is often nonoliguric (milder injury), where residual tubular function allows some Na conservation, keeping FENa <1%. This is one of the exceptions to the FENa rule.
⚠ Trap: FENa <1% leads students to diagnose prerenal AKI and give aggressive fluids without considering ATN from rhabdomyolysis. The board keyword is dipstick blood positive + zero RBCs on microscopy = myoglobinuria.
Trap 05 Dipstick Negative = No Proteinuria? Not in Myeloma.
A 67-year-old man presents with fatigue, back pain, and a creatinine of 3.0 mg/dL. Urine dipstick shows trace protein. His serum calcium is 11.8 mg/dL and total protein is 9.2 g/dL while albumin is 3.1 g/dL. What is the most appropriate next step to evaluate his proteinuria?
Correct Answer: Urine protein electrophoresis (UPEP) — to detect Bence-Jones proteins (monoclonal light chains) not detected by dipstick.
The dipstick detects albumin only. Light chains from plasma cell dyscrasias (multiple myeloma) pass through the glomerulus and overwhelm proximal tubule reabsorption, resulting in massive proteinuria — which is entirely missed by dipstick. The clue here is elevated total protein with relatively normal albumin (=high globulin gap), hypercalcemia, and renal failure in a middle-aged/older male.
⚠ Trap: Trusting the dipstick as the gold standard for proteinuria screening. Board keyword: elevated protein-albumin gap + normal dipstick + AKI = UPEP for Bence-Jones proteins.
Trap 06 Obstruction Without Hydronephrosis
A 58-year-old woman with known retroperitoneal fibrosis develops oliguria and rising creatinine over 5 days. Renal ultrasound shows no hydronephrosis and normal-appearing kidneys. Has obstruction been ruled out?
Correct Answer: No — obstruction has NOT been ruled out. A MAG3 furosemide renogram is required.
In retroperitoneal fibrosis (and tumor encasement of the ureters), the ureters cannot dilate in response to obstruction — so the classic sign of hydronephrosis is absent even with complete obstruction. Normal renal ultrasound in this clinical context is falsely reassuring. The next step is a MAG3 nuclear medicine renogram to look for obstructive uropathy.
⚠ Trap: Accepting the normal ultrasound as proof of no obstruction. Board keyword: retroperitoneal fibrosis / encasing tumor = ultrasound cannot rule out obstruction → MAG3 renogram.
Trap 07 Eosinophiluria — No Longer Recommended
A 45-year-old woman on amoxicillin for a urinary tract infection develops rising creatinine, mild proteinuria, hematuria, and pyuria 2 weeks into treatment. You suspect allergic interstitial nephritis. Which diagnostic test should you order to confirm?
Correct Answer: Renal biopsy — NOT urine eosinophils.
Urine eosinophiluria (>10% eosinophils on Wright's or Hansel's stain) was classically taught as diagnostic for allergic interstitial nephritis. However, urinary eosinophils can be elevated in many other causes of AKI (including cholesterol emboli, UTI, pyelonephritis). The test has no diagnostic utility and is no longer recommended in the workup of AKI. Renal biopsy remains the gold standard.
⚠ Trap: Ordering urine eosinophils as a confirmatory test for interstitial nephritis. Board keyword: eosinophiluria is NOT useful or recommended for AKI workup — biopsy is needed.
Trap 08 Water Diuresis vs Solute Diuresis — The Osmolality Pivot
Two patients both have urine output of 4 L/day. Patient A has urine osmolality of 80 mosmol/L. Patient B has urine osmolality of 380 mosmol/L. Patient B's primary diagnosis is __? and the most important next diagnostic step is __?
Correct Answer: Patient B has a solute diuresis. The next step is to identify the responsible solute (glucose/urea/mannitol/Na).
A urine osmolality >300 mosmol/L with >3 L/day = solute diuresis — the diluting capacity of the kidney is overwhelmed by the osmotic load. The most common cause is poorly controlled diabetes with glucosuria. With solute diuresis, more water than sodium may be lost (if the solute is non-electrolyte, e.g., glucose) → hypernatremia. Patient A (Uosm 80) = water diuresis → AVP axis problem.
⚠ Trap: Treating all polyuria as DI without checking urine osmolality first. Board keyword: urine osmolality is the pivot point — always check it before ordering AVP/copeptin tests.
Trap 09 RBC Casts with No Azotemia — Biopsy Still Indicated
A 24-year-old woman presents with gross hematuria and mild periorbital edema 2 weeks after a pharyngeal infection. Her creatinine is 0.9 mg/dL (normal), but urine microscopy shows dysmorphic RBCs and RBC casts. Urine protein:creatinine ratio is 1.4. What is the most appropriate next step?
Correct Answer: Serologic evaluation + renal biopsy — even though the creatinine is currently normal.
RBC casts indicate active glomerular disease regardless of current GFR. The pathologic pattern (e.g., poststreptococcal GN, IgA nephropathy, rapidly progressive GN) has critical implications for prognosis and treatment. Waiting for azotemia to develop before pursuing biopsy is a critical error — by that time, irreversible scarring may have occurred.
⚠ Trap: Reassurance because creatinine is "normal." Board keyword: RBC casts = indication for biopsy regardless of creatinine level.
Trap 10 Anemia + Hypocalcemia Alone Cannot Diagnose CKD vs AKI
A 52-year-old man presents with creatinine 5.2 mg/dL. He has hemoglobin 9.1, calcium 7.4, and phosphorus 6.8 mg/dL. A colleague says: "These labs confirm chronic kidney disease — he has anemia of CKD and renal osteodystrophy." Is this interpretation correct?
Correct Answer: No — anemia, hypocalcemia, and hyperphosphatemia are also frequently present in AKI and cannot distinguish acute from chronic kidney disease.
These metabolic abnormalities reflect the degree of GFR reduction and can develop rapidly in severe AKI. The correct approach: use history (prior creatinine values), urinalysis, and renal ultrasound. Small kidneys with increased echogenicity + cortical thinning = chronic. Normal-sized kidneys with intact parenchyma + recent creatinine rise = acute or acute-on-chronic. Renal osteodystrophy on x-ray is the only truly specific late finding for CKD (but requires ESRD on dialysis).
⚠ Trap: Using metabolic labs alone to stage acute vs chronic. Board keyword: small kidneys on ultrasound = the best single indicator of chronicity. Anemia and hypocalcemia do NOT distinguish.
