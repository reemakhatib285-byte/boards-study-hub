01

The Diagnosis — ACS Spectrum & NSTEMI Criteria

ACS is divided by the presenting ECG into two main groups — this determines the urgency and direction of management. Within NSTE-ACS, the troponin result distinguishes NSTEMI from unstable angina.

The ACS Spectrum

UA — Unstable Angina
  • No ST elevation on ECG
  • Troponin negative
  • No myocyte necrosis
  • Proportion falling — hsTn reclassifies many to NSTEMI
NSTEMI ← board focus
  • No ST elevation on ECG
  • Troponin positive (dynamic rise/fall)
  • Myocyte necrosis confirmed
  • Incidence rising: obesity, DM, CKD, aging + hsTn adoption
STEMI — different pathway
  • ST elevation on ECG
  • Complete thrombotic occlusion typical
  • Immediate reperfusion priority
  • Incidence declining

NSTEMI Diagnostic Criteria — All Three Required

Establish the Diagnosis
  • Clinical features of ACS: chest pain at rest, recent onset (<2 weeks), or crescendo pattern
  • No ST elevation on ECG
  • Elevated cardiac troponin — dynamic rise and/or fall — no other explanation

ECG Findings in NSTEMI

What You See
  • ST depression in ~⅓ of patients
  • T-wave changes (less specific)
  • New deep T-wave inversions ≥0.3 mV → highly specific
  • Normal ECG — does not rule out NSTEMI
🚨 Board Alert

Normal ECG does not exclude NSTEMI. The troponin is the diagnostic biomarker. "Normal ECG → safe to discharge" is a classic board trap — do not fall for it.

Cardiac Troponin — The Key Biomarker

High-Sensitivity Troponin (hsTn)
  • Preferred biomarker — detects smaller degrees of myocyte injury
  • Dynamic pattern: rise AND/OR fall required for acute MI
  • A single elevated value may reflect chronic myocardial injury (not acute MI) — context matters
  • hsTn adoption has reclassified many previous UA diagnoses to NSTEMI
❌ What to Eliminate — Diagnosis Questions
✕
"Normal ECG rules out NSTEMI" → wrong; troponin is the biomarker; ECG is supportive only
✕
"NSTEMI and UA are the same" → wrong; NSTEMI = positive troponin = necrosis = different management tier
✕
"Troponin negative → UA → conservative" → check timing; a single early negative troponin needs serial repeat before ruling out MI

Invasive Strategy & Timing

Once NSTEMI is confirmed, the critical question is: how fast to the catheterisation lab? Haemodynamic stability does not change whether to go invasive — only when. Confirmed NSTEMI always goes to cath.

Three-Tier Urgency System

< 2 h
Immediate Invasive — Very High Risk
  • Cardiogenic shock / haemodynamic instability
  • Acute HF due to ongoing ischaemia
  • Cardiac arrest post-presentation
  • Life-threatening arrhythmias (VT/VF)
  • Mechanical complications of MI
  • Angina refractory to maximal medical treatment
  • Recurrent dynamic ischaemic ECG changes
< 24 h
Early Invasive — High Risk
  • Confirmed NSTEMI (positive troponin) — this alone is sufficient for early invasive; stability does not override this
  • GRACE score >140
  • Transient ST elevation
  • Dynamic ST or T-wave changes on serial ECGs
Selective
Non-High Risk — Conservative First
  • None of the above criteria present
  • Non-invasive assessment first: stress test or CT coronary angiography
  • May proceed to angiography if non-invasive testing shows high-risk features

Decision Flowchart

Shock / Arrest / Refractory Ischaemia / Life-Threatening Arrhythmia?
↓ YES
IMMEDIATE — < 2 hours
↓ NO
Positive Troponin / GRACE >140 / Dynamic ECG / Transient STE?
↓ YES
EARLY INVASIVE — < 24 hours
↓ NO
SELECTIVE — Conservative + Non-Invasive Testing First
⚠ Board Trap 1 — "Stable" NSTEMI
Scenario: confirmed NSTEMI, HR 82, SBP 122, no acute HF — what is the next step?

Trap answer: "Repeat serial troponins at 6h and consider discharge if stable."

Why wrong: A positive troponin alone meets the early invasive (<24h) criterion. Haemodynamic stability moves you from <2h to <24h — it does NOT make management conservative. Confirmed NSTEMI = go to cath within 24h.

Correct answer: Early invasive strategy ≤24h.

Board keyword: Positive troponin = early invasive
❌ What to Eliminate — Timing Questions
✕
"Patient is stable → conservative management" → wrong; positive troponin mandates early invasive ≤24h regardless of stability
✕
"Serial troponins → discharge if stable" → wrong once troponin is already confirmed positive
✕
"Cardiogenic shock → <24h" → wrong; shock = <2h (immediate), not <24h
03

Beta Blocker Contraindications in NSTEMI

Beta blockers are standard early therapy in NSTEMI — they reduce heart rate, myocardial oxygen demand, and risk of arrhythmia. Target HR 50–60 bpm. The boards test two patterns: (1) identifying real contraindications; (2) not being fooled by things that look like contraindications but aren't.

Real vs Fake Contraindications

🚫 Real Contraindications — Do NOT Give
  • HR < 50 bpm
  • SBP < 90 mmHg
  • PR >0.24 sec
  • 2nd or 3rd degree AV block (without pacemaker)
  • Acute decompensated HF / cardiogenic shock (Killip III–IV) ← most important board trap
  • Severe reactive airways disease: asthma or severe COPD with active bronchospasm
✅ These Do NOT Contraindicate — Give the Drug
  • HR 60 → threshold is <50; HR 60 = safe to give
  • SBP 110 → threshold is <90; SBP 110 = safe to give
  • Interstitial lung disease (ILD) → NOT a contraindication. ILD is restrictive, not obstructive — zero β2-mediated bronchospasm risk. Only bronchospastic airways disease (asthma/COPD) matters
  • COPD in general → only severe bronchospastic COPD is a contraindication; mild COPD with cardioselective BB is acceptable

Killip Classification — Why It Decides the Beta Blocker Decision

Killip Class and Beta Blocker Safety
KillipClinical SignsBeta Blocker
INo signs of HF✓ Give
IIRales <50% lung fields, S3 gallop✓ Give (cautiously, monitor closely)
IIIPulmonary oedema — rales >50% lung fields✕ Contraindicated
IVCardiogenic shock✕ Contraindicated

Why: beta blockers ↓ contractility. In an already failing ventricle (Killip III–IV), this precipitates or worsens cardiogenic shock. Once HF/shock resolves, beta blockers can be (re)started before discharge.

⚠ Board Trap 2 — Pulmonary Rales + NSTEMI
Scenario: NSTEMI, bilateral basal rales, HR 92, SBP 118 — next step?

Trap answer: "HR and BP are within acceptable range — start beta blockers."

Why wrong: Bilateral rales = acute decompensated HF = Killip III. HR and BP thresholds are not the only contraindications. Beta blockers in Killip III can precipitate cardiogenic shock.

Correct answer: Beta blockers contraindicated. Treat the HF first.

Board keyword: Bilateral rales = Killip III = contraindication
⚠ Board Trap 3 — HR 60 or ILD
Scenario: NSTEMI, HR 60, known interstitial lung disease — give beta blockers?

Trap answer: "HR is borderline low — withhold" or "ILD — avoid beta blockers."

Why wrong: The HR threshold is <50, not <60. ILD is a restrictive, not obstructive, lung disease — there is no β2-bronchospasm risk. Neither is a contraindication.

Correct answer: Give beta blockers.

Board keyword: ILD ≠ bronchospastic; HR threshold = <50
❌ What to Eliminate — Beta Blocker Questions
✕
"Withhold because HR 60" → wrong; threshold is HR <50
✕
"ILD contraindicates beta blockers" → wrong; only bronchospastic disease (asthma/COPD) matters; ILD is restrictive
✕
"SBP 110 is too low to give beta blockers" → wrong; threshold is SBP <90
✕
"HR and BP are fine — start beta blockers despite bilateral rales" → wrong; rales = Killip III = absolute contraindication regardless of HR/BP
04

Nitrates — Use & Contraindications

Nitrates reduce myocardial oxygen demand via venodilation (↓ preload) and mild arterial dilation (↓ afterload at higher doses). Effective for symptom relief in most NSTEMI — but in specific conditions, the same mechanism causes catastrophic haemodynamic collapse.

The Mechanism Behind All the Traps

Normal Heart — Safe

Nitrates → ↓ venous return → ↓ preload → ↓ LVEDP → ↓ myocardial O₂ demand

CO compensates via ↑ HR and ↑ contractility. Therapeutic.

Fixed Output States — Catastrophic

Severe AS: fixed valve obstruction → CO cannot increase → ↓ preload → ↓↓ output → severe hypotension

RV infarct: RV output entirely preload-dependent → ↓ venous return → RV failure → shock

Absolute Contraindications

🚫 Do NOT Give Nitrates
  • SBP < 90 mmHg — will worsen hypotension
  • PDE-5 inhibitor use:
    • Sildenafil / Vardenafil → within 24h
    • Tadalafil → within 48h (longer half-life)
  • Right ventricular infarct — RV preload-dependent; drop preload → RV failure → shock
  • Severe aortic stenosis — fixed outflow obstruction; drop preload → catastrophic hypotension
💡 Why PDE-5 Inhibitors Are Dangerous With Nitrates

Nitrates work via cGMP → vasodilation. PDE-5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil) block cGMP breakdown. Together: additive, refractory vasodilation → severe, life-threatening hypotension.

Tadalafil has a longer half-life than sildenafil/vardenafil → 48h washout required vs 24h.

⚠ Board Trap 4 — Severe Aortic Stenosis + NSTEMI
Scenario: 72F, DM, HTN, severe AS on echo, rising troponin — which drug will cause deterioration?

Trap answer: "CCB" — because AS involves obstruction so avoid negative inotropes.

Why wrong: CCBs are not specifically contraindicated in aortic stenosis. The dangerous drug is nitrates — they reduce preload and afterload against a fixed obstruction. Even small doses cause catastrophic hypotension in severe AS. Ticagrelor (standard antithrombotic) is fine. Aspirin is fine.

Correct answer: Nitrates are the dangerous drug in severe AS + NSTEMI.

Board keyword: Fixed obstruction + nitrates = catastrophic hypotension
❌ What to Eliminate — Nitrate Questions
✕
"CCB is dangerous in severe AS" → wrong; nitrates are the dangerous drug in AS
✕
"Ticagrelor is harmful in severe AS" → wrong; standard antithrombotic, no contraindication in AS
✕
"Sildenafil was 3 days ago — safe to give nitrates" → wrong; tadalafil requires 48h washout; always ask about ALL PDE-5 inhibitors
✕
"RV infarct — give sublingual NTG for chest pain" → wrong; RV entirely preload-dependent; nitrates → venous pooling → RV failure → shock

Vasospastic Angina (Prinzmetal / Variant Angina)

A completely different pathophysiology from atherosclerotic ACS. The coronary arteries transiently spasm — ST elevation appears on ECG during the episode, mimicking STEMI, but resolves spontaneously. The key insight: the treatment is the opposite of what you'd reach for in standard ACS.

The Diagnostic Triad — Memorize All Three

Vasospastic Angina = All Three Present
  • Transient ST elevation at rest during episodes → resolves spontaneously (not persistent like STEMI)
  • Negative troponin — no necrosis unless spasm is prolonged
  • Normal or near-normal coronary arteries on angiography

Typical patient: younger, fewer classical cardiac risk factors. Single risk factor most strongly associated: cigarette smoking. More common in Japan.

How to Distinguish from STEMI

STEMI
  • ST elevation persists
  • Troponin rises
  • Obstructive disease on angiography
  • Requires immediate PCI
Vasospastic Angina
  • ST elevation resolves spontaneously
  • Troponin negative
  • Normal coronaries on angiography
  • Treat with CCB — no emergency cath

Treatment — Use, Avoid, and Why

✅ First Line
  • CCBs — first line: verapamil, diltiazem, amlodipine
  • Nitrates — effective acutely; long-term use limited by tolerance
  • Smoking cessation
❌ Avoid
  • Aspirin — inhibits prostacyclin → worsens spasm
  • Beta blockers — unhelpful; may worsen via unopposed α-mediated vasoconstriction
  • NSAIDs — same mechanism as aspirin → harmful
💡 Why Aspirin Worsens Vasospasm

COX-1 inhibition blocks both thromboxane A₂ (proconstrictor) AND prostacyclin (potent vasodilator). In vasospasm, the loss of prostacyclin protection dominates → worsened coronary vasoconstriction.

NSAIDs share this same mechanism.

⚠ Board Trap 5 — Young Patient, ST Elevation at Rest, Negative Troponin, Normal Coronaries
Scenario: 38M, smoker, chest pain at rest 3am, transient ST elevation on ECG, troponin negative, coronaries normal on angiography

Trap answers: "Give NSAIDs for pain" / "Start beta blockers to prevent further episodes" / "Emergency PCI — it's a STEMI."

Why wrong: NSAIDs and aspirin → block prostacyclin → worsen vasospasm. Beta blockers → unopposed α-adrenergic vasoconstriction → may worsen spasm. Emergency PCI: troponin negative + transient STE + normal coronaries = vasospasm, not STEMI. Emergent cath already showed normal arteries.

Correct answer: Calcium channel blocker — first line for vasospastic angina.

Board keyword: Transient STE + negative troponin + normal coronaries = CCB
❌ What to Eliminate — Vasospastic Angina Questions
✕
"NSAIDs for the pain" → wrong; inhibit prostacyclin → worsen coronary vasospasm
✕
"Beta blockers to prevent spasm" → wrong; unhelpful; unopposed α → may worsen spasm
✕
"Emergency PCI — ST elevation" → wrong; transient STE + negative troponin + normal coronaries = vasospasm; not STEMI
✕
"Aspirin is the cornerstone of all ACS" → wrong here; aspirin worsens coronary vasospasm via prostacyclin inhibition

Anticoagulants in NSTE-ACS

Four parenteral anticoagulants are used in NSTE-ACS. The board tested the fondaparinux vs enoxaparin comparison twice. There is exactly one thing fondaparinux does better — and the board specifically traps you into saying the wrong thing.

The Four Options — Board-Focused Comparison

DrugClass / MechanismEfficacy vs EnoxaparinBleeding vs EnoxaparinBoard Point
UFH Indirect thrombin + Xa inhibitor Equivalent Similar Standard in cath lab; requires aPTT monitoring
Enoxaparin (LMWH) Indirect Xa > thrombin inhibitor Reference Reference Preferred conservative strategy; add UFH if last dose >8h before PCI
Bivalirudin Direct thrombin inhibitor Equivalent Less than UFH Used selectively peri-PCI; not clearly superior to heparin in efficacy
Fondaparinux Indirect factor Xa inhibitor only Equivalent Less — safest profile Needs supplemental UFH at PCI — catheter thrombosis risk

Why Fondaparinux Needs Supplemental UFH at PCI

💡 The Catheter Thrombosis Problem

Fondaparinux only inhibits factor Xa. During PCI, the catheter surface itself triggers thrombin generation through contact activation — a pathway requiring direct thrombin inhibition or a broader-spectrum anticoagulant.

Without supplemental UFH at PCI, there is a significant risk of catheter thrombosis. This is a well-established clinical constraint of fondaparinux.

⚠ Board Trap 6 — Fondaparinux vs Enoxaparin Advantage
Q: What is the advantage of fondaparinux over enoxaparin in NSTE-ACS?

Trap answer: "Superior efficacy in preventing ischaemic events / thrombosis."

Why wrong: Efficacy is equivalent. The one and only advantage of fondaparinux is less bleeding. Do not conflate safety with efficacy — the board specifically tests this distinction.

Correct answer: Same efficacy as enoxaparin, fewer bleeding complications.

Board keyword: Fondaparinux = same efficacy, less bleeding — not better efficacy
❌ What to Eliminate — Anticoagulant Questions
✕
"Fondaparinux has better efficacy than enoxaparin" → wrong; efficacy is equivalent; the advantage is less bleeding only
✕
"Fondaparinux is safe to use alone throughout PCI" → wrong; needs supplemental UFH at PCI to prevent catheter thrombosis
✕
"Bivalirudin is clearly superior to heparin" → wrong; similar efficacy; used selectively peri-PCI for its bleeding profile advantage

DAPT After Drug-Eluting Stent

Drug-eluting stents (DES) reduce restenosis compared to bare-metal stents, but the drug coating delays endothelialisation — exposed stent struts remain in contact with blood for longer. Without DAPT, late in-stent thrombosis is a catastrophic risk. The mechanism: platelet-mediated thrombosis on exposed metal → DAPT required, not anticoagulation.

Standard Regimen

Post-DES DAPT — The Answer

Aspirin + Prasugrel (preferred post-PCI) or Ticagrelor — for 12 months

  • Aspirin alone = insufficient; no P2Y12 = high in-stent thrombosis risk
  • P2Y12 alone (no aspirin) = incomplete DAPT
  • Both components are required for 12 months post-DES

Why Prasugrel is Preferred Over Clopidogrel Post-PCI

✅ Prasugrel Advantages
  • Faster onset, stronger, more consistent irreversible P2Y12 inhibition
  • Not CYP2C19-dependent → no poor-responder problem
  • Loading dose: 60 mg → maintenance: 10 mg/day
  • Reduce to 5 mg/day if <60 kg or >75 years (↑ bleeding risk)
  • Give only after coronary anatomy is known (post-angiography) — if patient needs CABG, prasugrel must be held ≥7 days
⚠ Prasugrel Contraindications
  • Prior stroke or TIA — markedly increased intracranial bleeding risk
  • High bleeding risk

In these cases: use ticagrelor or clopidogrel instead

💡 Why Not Clopidogrel as Default Post-PCI

Up to 1/3 of patients carry a CYP2C19 loss-of-function variant → inadequate conversion of clopidogrel to its active metabolite → poor platelet inhibition. Post-DES, this means the stent is not adequately covered against platelet-mediated thrombosis.

Prasugrel and ticagrelor do not depend on CYP2C19 → predictable, reliable platelet inhibition in all patients.

Triple Therapy — When It's Needed

Post-DES Patient Who Also Has AF (Needing Anticoagulation)

Aspirin + P2Y12 + anticoagulant = "triple therapy" = major bleeding risk.

Current practice: minimise triple therapy duration. Use NOAC + single antiplatelet (usually clopidogrel) for most of the DAPT period. Full triple therapy only for the shortest required period.

⚠ Board Trap 7 — Anticoagulant as DAPT Post-DES
Scenario: NSTEMI, DES placed, ready for discharge — which medications?

Trap answers: "Aspirin + Dabigatran" / "Aspirin + Warfarin" / "Clopidogrel alone" / "Aspirin alone."

Why wrong: Dabigatran and warfarin are anticoagulants — they prevent fibrin-mediated clot but do NOT prevent platelet-mediated thrombosis. DES thrombosis is driven by platelet activation on exposed metal struts. Only antiplatelet drugs (aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor) address this mechanism. Anticoagulants are added only if a separate indication exists (e.g. AF, mechanical valve).

Correct answer: Aspirin + Prasugrel (or Ticagrelor) for 12 months.

Board keyword: DES thrombosis = platelet-mediated → antiplatelet, not anticoagulant
❌ What to Eliminate — DAPT Questions
✕
"Aspirin + Dabigatran" → wrong; dabigatran is an anticoagulant, does not prevent platelet-mediated in-stent thrombosis
✕
"Aspirin + Warfarin" → wrong; same reason; add anticoagulant only if separate indication (AF, mechanical valve)
✕
"Clopidogrel alone" → wrong; no aspirin = incomplete DAPT; also clopidogrel is inferior to prasugrel post-PCI (CYP2C19 resistance)
✕
"Aspirin alone" → wrong; without P2Y12 inhibition, high in-stent thrombosis risk

Exam Traps — Board Exam Coach

All 7 tested traps plus additional traps derived from mechanisms tested in the questions. For each: the vignette, the distractor, the correct answer, the mechanism, and the board keyword.

⚠ Trap 1 — Confirmed NSTEMI, Haemodynamically Stable
Vignette: 64M, chest pain ×3h, troponin positive, no ST elevation, HR 85, BP 128/80, no rales. What is the next step?

Distractor: "Patient is haemodynamically stable — serial troponins at 6h and consider discharge if stable."

Why it's tempting: The patient looks fine. No shock. No HF. Seems like watchful waiting is safe.

Why it's wrong: A positive troponin alone = confirmed NSTEMI = early invasive ≤24h criterion met. Haemodynamic stability shifts the tier from <2h to <24h — it does not justify conservative management. Once the troponin is positive, the management pathway is set.

Correct answer: Early invasive strategy ≤24 hours.

Board keyword: Positive troponin = early invasive — stability only delays, doesn't cancel
⚠ Trap 2 — NSTEMI + Bilateral Rales
Vignette: 70F, NSTEMI, bilateral basal crepitations, HR 95, SBP 118. Is it safe to start beta blockers?

Distractor: "HR and BP are within acceptable limits — start beta blockers for rate control and cardioprotection."

Why it's tempting: HR 95 (<100) and SBP 118 (>90) are both within the commonly quoted safe thresholds.

Why it's wrong: Bilateral rales = pulmonary oedema = Killip III = acute decompensated HF. This is an absolute contraindication regardless of HR and BP. Beta blockers ↓ contractility — adding them to a failing ventricle can precipitate cardiogenic shock.

Correct answer: Beta blockers are contraindicated. Treat the heart failure first.

Board keyword: Bilateral rales = Killip III = contraindication; HR/BP thresholds are not the only criteria
⚠ Trap 3 — NSTEMI + HR 60 + ILD
Vignette: 58M, NSTEMI, HR 60, known interstitial lung disease on CT. Should beta blockers be withheld?

Distractor: "HR 60 is borderline low — hold beta blockers" OR "ILD is a lung disease — beta blockers carry bronchospasm risk."

Why it's tempting: Low-ish HR. A lung disease. Both seem like reasons to be cautious.

Why it's wrong: HR threshold for beta blocker contraindication is <50 bpm. HR 60 is safe. ILD is a restrictive interstitial disease — no airway obstruction, no β2-receptor-mediated bronchospasm risk. Only obstructive airways disease (asthma, severe COPD with active bronchospasm) contraindicates beta blockers.

Correct answer: Give beta blockers. Neither HR 60 nor ILD is a contraindication.

Board keyword: ILD = restrictive = no bronchospasm risk; HR threshold = <50, not <60
⚠ Trap 4 — Severe Aortic Stenosis + NSTEMI — Which Drug to Avoid
Vignette: 78F, DM, HTN, severe AS (valve area 0.7 cm²), rising troponin, SBP 100. Which drug will cause the most deterioration?

Distractor: "CCB — negatively inotropic, avoid in obstructive valve disease."

Why it's tempting: Severe AS with fixed obstruction — negative inotropes seem dangerous.

Why it's wrong: CCBs are not specifically contraindicated in AS. The truly dangerous drug is nitrates. Fixed valve area means CO cannot increase to compensate for ↓ preload. Nitrates → ↓ venous return → ↓ preload → ↓ CO → catastrophic hypotension. Even small doses are dangerous.

Correct answer: Nitrates are contraindicated in severe aortic stenosis.

Board keyword: Fixed obstruction + nitrates = no CO compensation = catastrophic hypotension
⚠ Trap 5 — Young Patient, ST Elevation at Rest, Negative Troponin, Normal Coronaries
Vignette: 35M, smoker, chest pain waking him at 3am, transient ST elevation in inferior leads on ECG, troponin negative ×2, coronary angiography shows normal arteries.

Distractor: "Start aspirin and beta blocker for secondary prevention" / "NSAIDs for pain" / "Emergency PCI — ST elevation."

Why it's tempting: ST elevation = STEMI reflex. Aspirin = every ACS. Beta blockers = every ACS.

Why it's wrong: This is vasospastic (Prinzmetal's) angina. Transient STE + negative troponin + normal coronaries = spasm, not fixed plaque. Aspirin and NSAIDs inhibit prostacyclin → worsen vasospasm. Beta blockers → unopposed α-vasoconstriction → may worsen spasm. Emergency PCI is wrong — coronaries are normal.

Correct answer: Calcium channel blocker (e.g. diltiazem, amlodipine) — first-line treatment.

Board keyword: Transient STE + neg troponin + normal coronaries = CCB, not aspirin/BB/PCI
⚠ Trap 6 — Fondaparinux Advantage Over Enoxaparin
Q: A patient with NSTE-ACS is started on fondaparinux instead of enoxaparin. What is the primary reason for this choice?

Distractor: "Fondaparinux has superior anticoagulant efficacy — it prevents more thrombotic events."

Why it's tempting: If you choose a different drug, it must be because it works better, right?

Why it's wrong: Fondaparinux has equivalent efficacy to enoxaparin. The one and only clinical advantage is less bleeding. Also important: fondaparinux requires supplemental UFH at the time of PCI to prevent catheter thrombosis (fondaparinux only blocks Xa, not thrombin generated by catheter contact activation).

Correct answer: Less bleeding. Efficacy is equivalent to enoxaparin, not superior.

Board keyword: Fondaparinux = same efficacy, LESS bleeding — never "better efficacy"
⚠ Trap 7 — Post-DES: Anticoagulant vs Antiplatelet
Vignette: 60M, NSTEMI, DES placed successfully. On discharge, which antiplatelet regimen is correct?

Distractor: "Aspirin + Dabigatran" or "Aspirin + Warfarin" — because the stent is a clotting risk.

Why it's tempting: Stent = clotting risk → use an anticoagulant. Logic seems sound.

Why it's wrong: DES thrombosis is platelet-mediated — platelets activate on exposed metal struts. Anticoagulants (dabigatran, warfarin) prevent fibrin-based clot but do NOT prevent platelet aggregation. Only antiplatelet therapy addresses the in-stent thrombosis mechanism. Dabigatran/warfarin are only added if a separate indication exists (AF, mechanical valve).

Correct answer: Aspirin + Prasugrel (or Ticagrelor) for 12 months.

Board keyword: DES thrombosis = platelet-driven = antiplatelet not anticoagulant
