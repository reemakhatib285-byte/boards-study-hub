ECG Localisation & Prognosis

The location of the STEMI determines the culprit vessel, autonomic tone, the type of AV block if it develops, and the prognosis. Inferior vs anterior is one of the highest-yield distinctions on the boards.

Inferior vs Anterior — The Master Comparison

Inferior STEMI
  • Leads: II, III, aVF
  • Culprit vessel: RCA (usually), occasionally LCx
  • Autonomic tone: Vagal dominance → bradycardia, hypotension
  • AV block mechanism: Adenosine + vagal — not structural necrosis
  • AV block QRS: Narrow — His-Purkinje intact
  • AV block prognosis: Transient, usually resolves spontaneously
  • Treatment of block: Atropine if symptomatic; usually no pacing
  • Overall prognosis: Better
Anterior STEMI
  • Leads: V1–V4
  • Culprit vessel: LAD
  • Autonomic tone: Sympathetic → tachycardia, hypertension
  • AV block mechanism: Ischaemic necrosis of the conduction system — structural
  • AV block QRS: Wide — infranodal, below bifurcation of His
  • AV block prognosis: Often does not resolve; may be permanent
  • Treatment of block: Temporary pacing; often permanent pacing needed
  • Overall prognosis: Worse — larger territory, more LV dysfunction

ECG Territory → Vessel → Mechanism → Outcome

II, III, aVF elevation → RCA / LCx
↓
Adenosine / Vagal AV block → Narrow QRS → Transient
↓
Good prognosis — no prophylactic antiarrhythmics
V1–V4 elevation → LAD
↓
Conduction necrosis → Wide QRS → Often permanent
↓
Worse prognosis — large territory, LV dysfunction

ST Elevation Diagnostic Criteria — Know the Sex-Specific Thresholds

LeadsPopulationRequired ST Elevation
All leads except V₂–V₃All patients≥ 0.1 mV (1 mm)
V₂–V₃Men ≥40 years≥ 0.2 mV (2 mm)
V₂–V₃Men <40 years≥ 0.25 mV (2.5 mm)
V₂–V₃Women (any age)≥ 0.15 mV (1.5 mm) — LOWER threshold
🚨 Women — Board Favourite

Women require only 1.5 mm in V₂–V₃ vs 2 mm for men ≥40. Applying the male threshold to women = systematic under-diagnosis. This is one of the most commonly tested board distinctions in STEMI.

⚠ Board Trap — Inferior STEMI Prognosis
Q: Which STEMI has worse prognosis? / Q: Patient with inferior STEMI + VPBs — start lidocaine?

Trap: "Inferior STEMI has worse prognosis" / "Give lidocaine prophylactically."

Why wrong: Inferior STEMI has better prognosis. Prophylactic lidocaine is absolutely contraindicated — multiple trials showed increased mortality. Never give prophylactic antiarrhythmics in STEMI.

Correct: Inferior = good prognosis. No prophylactic antiarrhythmics.

Board keyword: Inferior = vagal/adenosine = transient = good prognosis
❌ What to Eliminate — ECG Localisation Questions
✕
"Inferior STEMI has worse prognosis than anterior" → wrong; inferior has better prognosis
✕
"RCA occlusion → complete permanent AV block" → wrong; RCA AV block is vagal/adenosine = transient = resolves spontaneously
✕
"2 mm ST elevation threshold applies to women in V₂–V₃" → wrong; women need only 1.5 mm

Troponin & CK-MB Kinetics

Two biomarkers, two different jobs. Troponin diagnoses the MI. CK-MB detects reinfarction — because it clears in 48–72h while troponin stays elevated for 7–10 days.

Troponin Kinetics After STEMI

Troponin Timeline
  • Rises within hours of onset
  • Peaks at 12–24h (without reperfusion)
  • After reperfusion → earlier, higher peak (rapid washout from interstitium = reperfusion sign)
  • Remains elevated for 7–10 days after large MI — the board number
  • Criterion for AMI: rise and/or fall with ≥1 value above 99th percentile
CK-MB — The Reinfarction Marker
  • Returns to normal in 48–72 hours
  • Because troponin stays elevated 7–10 days, only CK-MB can detect reinfarction during that window
  • A new rise in CK-MB while troponin is still elevated = new infarct event
  • CK-MB/CK ratio ≥2.5 → suggests myocardial (not skeletal muscle) source

Visual Timeline

Troponin: ↗────────── PEAK 12–24h ────────────────→ Still elevated at day 7–10
CK-MB: ↗── PEAK (earlier) ──↘ Normal by 48–72h
↑ CK-MB while troponin still elevated = REINFARCTION
Reperfusion → washout → earlier/higher troponin peak = reperfusion sign
⚠ Board Trap — Troponin Duration
Q: For how long does troponin remain elevated after STEMI?

Trap answers: "48 hours" / "4 days" / "14 days."

Why wrong: 48h = CK-MB clearance. 14 days is too long. Troponin remains elevated for 7–10 days after a large MI.

Correct: 7–10 days.

Board keyword: Troponin = 7–10 days; CK-MB = 48–72h; reinfarction = ↑CK-MB
❌ What to Eliminate — Biomarker Questions
✕
"Troponin elevated for 48h" → wrong; 48h is CK-MB; troponin = 7–10 days
✕
"Use troponin to detect reinfarction" → wrong; troponin stays elevated 7–10d; CK-MB is the reinfarction marker
✕
"Reperfusion decreases the troponin peak" → wrong; reperfusion causes earlier AND higher peak (washout)

AV Block Post-MI

AV block after MI is one of the most testable dichotomies in cardiology boards. The mechanism, QRS width, prognosis, and management differ completely based on which vessel is occluded. Ask which wall first, then reason through it.

The Complete Comparison

FeatureInferior MI (RCA)Anterior MI (LAD)
MechanismVagal tone + adenosine releaseIschaemic necrosis of conduction tissue
QRS widthNarrow — His-Purkinje intact (supranodal)Wide — infranodal block below bifurcation of His
PrognosisGood — transient, reversiblePoor — extensive necrosis; often permanent
TreatmentAtropine if symptomatic; usually noneTemporary pacing (often permanent pacing)
Resolves?Yes, spontaneouslyOften does not
💜 Pearl — Why QRS Width Tells You Everything

Narrow QRS = block is at the AV node level (supranodal). His-Purkinje is intact below. Vagal/adenosine-mediated. Transient.

Wide QRS = infranodal block — in or below the bundle of His, where ischaemia has damaged the conduction tissue itself. Reflects extensive myocardial necrosis extending into the interventricular septum. Structural. Often permanent.

⚠ Board Trap — Bradycardia + Acute MI: Which Wall?
Scenario: acute MI + bradycardia + wide QRS AV block — what is the prognosis?

Framework when you see bradycardia + MI:

Step 1: Which wall? → Inferior (RCA) or Anterior (LAD)?

Step 2: What is the QRS width? → Narrow (vagal/adenosine) or Wide (structural necrosis)?

Inferior + narrow QRS → transient → good prognosis → atropine if needed.

Anterior + wide QRS → structural necrosis → often permanent → pacing required.

Board keyword: Wide QRS = infranodal = structural = poor; Narrow = vagal = transient = good
❌ What to Eliminate — AV Block Questions
✕
"RCA occlusion → permanent complete AV block" → wrong; RCA block is vagal/adenosine = transient = resolves
✕
"Anterior MI AV block has good prognosis" → wrong; LAD = conduction necrosis = wide QRS = often permanent = poor prognosis
✕
"Narrow QRS AV block → permanent pacing needed" → wrong; narrow QRS = supranodal = transient = atropine if needed

Initial Treatment Protocol

Know the standard STEMI protocol in order, and which drugs are contraindicated in which specific scenarios. The board gives you a patient with a complication (shock, RV infarct, PDE-5 inhibitor use) and asks what is or is not appropriate.

Standard Acute STEMI Protocol

📋 Give in Order
  1. Aspirin 160–325 mg chewed immediately (COX-1 → ↓TXA₂). Followed by 75–162 mg daily.
  2. P2Y₁₂ inhibitor (Prasugrel or Ticagrelor) — DAPT immediately
  3. UFH — anticoagulant of choice for primary PCI (60 U/kg bolus, max 4,000 U + 12 U/kg/h)
  4. Nitroglycerin — sublingual 0.4 mg, up to 3 doses; IV if persistent ischaemia. Check contraindications first.
  5. Beta blocker — if no contraindications, oral within 24h. IV only if specific indication; not routine.
  6. Oxygen — ONLY if SpO₂ <90%. Do NOT give routinely.

The Cardiogenic Shock Scenario

✅ Correct — STEMI + BP 80/60
  • UFH (heparin) — anticoagulant of choice; appropriate in shock; does not worsen haemodynamics
  • Proceed to primary PCI urgently
  • Aspirin + P2Y₁₂
❌ Do NOT Give in Shock
  • Nitrates → SBP <90 = absolute contraindication; will cause fatal hypotension
  • Fondaparinux alone → cannot be sole anticoagulant at PCI (catheter thrombosis risk; needs UFH)
  • Beta blockers → cardiogenic shock = Killip IV = contraindicated
  • Short-acting nifedipine → increases mortality in acute STEMI; never use

Nitrate Contraindications in STEMI

ContraindicationMechanism — Why Dangerous
SBP < 90 mmHgNitrates ↓ preload → worsens hypotension directly
RV infarctionClassic triad: inferior ECG + ↑JVP + clear lungs + hypotension. RV entirely preload-dependent; nitrates → venous pooling → RV failure → shock
Sildenafil / Vardenafil within 24hAdditive cGMP inhibition → profound, refractory hypotension
Tadalafil within 48hSame mechanism; longer half-life → 48h washout required

Calcium Antagonists in Acute STEMI

⚠ CCBs — No Role in Acute STEMI

Calcium antagonists are of little to no value in acute STEMI. Short-acting dihydropyridines (nifedipine) may be associated with increased mortality. Routine use is not recommended. This applies to both verapamil/diltiazem (non-DHP) and nifedipine (DHP).

⚠ Board Trap — Lidocaine for PVBs / Verapamil in STEMI
Scenario: STEMI + frequent PVBs on monitoring — which antiarrhythmic to start?

Trap 1: "Give IV lidocaine — prevents VF." → Wrong. Prophylactic lidocaine increases mortality. Contraindicated.

Trap 2: "Give verapamil in acute STEMI." → Wrong. Non-DHP CCBs have no role. Short-acting nifedipine increases mortality.

Trap 3: "Abciximab prevents arrhythmias." → Wrong. Abciximab is a GPIIb/IIIa inhibitor — it prevents platelet-mediated thrombosis, not arrhythmias.

Correct answer: Beta blocker — for VPBs and VF prevention. No prophylactic antiarrhythmic.

Board keyword: VPBs in STEMI = beta blocker; prophylactic lidocaine = contraindicated
❌ What to Eliminate — Acute Protocol Questions
✕
"Nitrates for chest pain in STEMI + BP 80/60" → wrong; SBP <90 = absolute nitrate contraindication
✕
"Oxygen routinely in all STEMI" → wrong; only if SpO₂ <90%
✕
"Fondaparinux alone at PCI" → wrong; needs supplemental UFH (catheter thrombosis risk)
✕
"Short-acting nifedipine" → wrong; increases mortality in acute STEMI; never use

Beta Blockers in STEMI

Start beta blockers orally within the first 24 hours in all eligible patients. Target HR 50–60 bpm. They reduce reinfarction, VF risk, and infarct size. IV beta blockers are no longer routine — risk of precipitating HF. The contraindications are identical to NSTEMI.

Real vs Fake Contraindications

🚫 Real Contraindications — Do NOT Give
  • PR > 0.24 sec — board-tested exact threshold
  • 2nd or 3rd degree AV block
  • HR < 50 bpm
  • SBP < 90 mmHg
  • Acute decompensated HF / Killip III–IV / cardiogenic shock
  • Severe reactive airways disease (asthma / severe COPD)
✅ These Do NOT Contraindicate
  • Tachycardia → actually a reason to use beta blockers
  • SBP 110 → threshold is <90; safe to give
  • HR 60 → threshold is <50; safe to give
  • ILD → only bronchospastic disease contraindicates; ILD is restrictive

IV Beta Blocker Protocol (if used)

IV Metoprolol — Conditions for Use

5 mg IV q2–5 min × 3 doses — only if: HR >60, SBP >100, PR <0.24s, no signs of acute HF

Note: IV beta blockers are no longer routinely recommended in STEMI due to risk of precipitating cardiogenic shock. Use oral route in first 24h when eligible.

Oral regimen: 50 mg q6h × 48h → then 100 mg q12h. Continue for at least 1 year post-STEMI.

❌ What to Eliminate — Beta Blocker Questions
✕
"Withhold because HR 60" → wrong; threshold is HR <50
✕
"ILD contraindicates beta blockers" → wrong; only bronchospastic disease (asthma/COPD) matters
✕
"Beta blockers for cardiogenic shock" → wrong; Killip IV = absolute contraindication; they reduce contractility
✕
"Routine IV beta blockers in all STEMI" → wrong; IV route no longer routine; risk of precipitating HF

Reperfusion Strategy

The overarching goal: minimize time from first medical contact (FMC) to initiation of reperfusion. Primary PCI is preferred. Fibrinolysis is the alternative when PCI cannot be achieved in time.

Time Goals

SettingGoalClass
PCI-capable hospital → cath labFMC-device ≤ 90 minClass I
Non-PCI hospital → transfer for PCIFMC-device ≤ 120 minClass I
Door-In Door-Out (transfer hospital)≤ 30 minTarget
Fibrinolysis (when PCI not achievable)Door-to-needle ≤ 30 minClass I
Post-fibrinolysis angiography (if successful)3–24 hoursTransfer to PCI center

When to Prefer PCI vs Fibrinolysis

✅ Prefer Primary PCI When:
  • Can be performed within 90–120 min of FMC
  • Diagnosis is in doubt
  • Cardiogenic shock is present
  • High bleeding risk (fibrinolysis too risky)
  • Symptoms present >2–3 hours (clot less easily lysed)
Fibrinolysis — When PCI Not Available in Time
  • FMC-device time anticipated >120 min
  • Goal: TIMI grade 3 flow (full perfusion with normal flow)
  • Post-fibrinolysis: transfer to PCI center; angiography 3–24h later
  • Failed reperfusion (ST still elevated at 90 min) → rescue PCI
  • Do NOT do angiography in first 2–3h after fibrinolysis

Fibrinolysis Absolute Contraindications — Must Know All

🚫 Absolute Contraindications to Fibrinolysis
  • Prior cerebrovascular hemorrhage at any time
  • Nonhemorrhagic stroke or CVE within past 1 year
  • Marked hypertension: SBP >180 mmHg and/or DBP >110 mmHg
  • Suspicion of aortic dissection
  • Active internal bleeding (excluding menses)

Multivessel Disease — A Critical Distinction

STEMI WITHOUT Cardiogenic Shock

PCI of nonculprit vessels — either during index procedure OR within 45 days — reduces CV events and need for repeat revascularization.

Evidence: PRAMI, CvLPRIT, COMPLETE trials

STEMI WITH Cardiogenic Shock

Routine PCI of nonculprit arteries during the index procedure is CONTRAINDICATED. Associated with increased rate of death or severe renal failure vs culprit-only PCI.

Rule: In shock → PCI the culprit only

⚠ Board Trap — Nonculprit PCI in Cardiogenic Shock
Scenario: STEMI + cardiogenic shock + culprit LAD stented successfully + high-grade RCA lesion — stent the RCA too?

Trap: "Complete revascularization is better — stent both vessels."

Why wrong: Complete revascularization applies only to STEMI without shock. In cardiogenic shock, nonculprit PCI during index procedure is contraindicated — associated with death and renal failure.

Correct: Culprit-only PCI when cardiogenic shock is present.

Board keyword: Shock + nonculprit PCI = contraindicated; no-shock = complete revascularization beneficial

DAPT — Thrombosis vs Restenosis

The single most important conceptual distinction in post-stent pharmacology. DAPT prevents thrombosis. It does NOT prevent restenosis. These are completely different processes — different mechanisms, timelines, and treatments. The boards love to blur this line.

Two Completely Different Problems

In-Stent Thrombosis
  • Mechanism: Platelet activation on exposed stent struts
  • Risk: 1–3% of all stents
  • Timing: Acute (<24h), subacute (1–30d), late (1–12m), very late (>1yr)
  • Consequences: Death 10–20%, MI 30–70% — catastrophic
  • Key cause: Premature DAPT discontinuation → 3–9× ↑ risk (especially first month)
  • Prevented by: DAPT (aspirin + P2Y₁₂)
  • DES vs BMS: DES has MORE late/very late thrombosis (first-generation DES)
Restenosis
  • Mechanism: Smooth muscle cell proliferation — neointimal hyperplasia (NOT a clot)
  • Rates: Balloon alone 20–50% → BMS 10–30% → DES 5–15%
  • DES drug: Everolimus, sirolimus, paclitaxel — blocks neointimal hyperplasia
  • Presentation: Recurrent angina within 12 months (gradual, not sudden death)
  • NOT prevented by: DAPT
  • Prevented by: DES drug coating
  • DES vs BMS: DES has LESS restenosis (5–15% vs 10–30%)

Post-DES Regimen

💊 Standard Post-DES DAPT

Aspirin + Prasugrel (or Ticagrelor) for 12 months

  • Prasugrel: loading 60 mg → maintenance 10 mg/day (5 mg/day if <60 kg or >75 years)
  • Contraindicated if prior stroke or TIA
  • Give only after coronary anatomy known (post-angiography)
  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, dabigatran) are NOT DAPT — only add for separate indication (AF, valve)
⚠ Board Traps — DAPT and Restenosis (Multiple)
Q: What does DAPT prevent after DES placement?

Trap 1: "DAPT prevents restenosis." → Wrong. DAPT prevents thrombosis. Restenosis = neointimal hyperplasia = prevented by the DES drug coating. Completely different mechanism.

Trap 2: "Stopping DAPT early raises restenosis risk." → Wrong mechanism. Stopping DAPT raises thrombosis risk 3–9×. No effect on restenosis.

Trap 3: "DES has more restenosis than BMS." → Wrong. DES has LESS restenosis (5–15% vs 10–30%). DES has more late thrombosis.

Trap 4: "Aspirin + Coumadin / Dabigatran post-DES." → Wrong. Anticoagulants prevent fibrin clot, not platelet-mediated thrombosis.

Board keyword: DAPT = thrombosis prevention, NOT restenosis; stopping DAPT = ↑thrombosis, not ↑restenosis
❌ What to Eliminate — DAPT Questions
✕
"DAPT prevents restenosis" → wrong; DAPT prevents thrombosis; restenosis = DES drug coating
✕
"Stopping DAPT → ↑ restenosis risk" → wrong mechanism; stopping DAPT → ↑ thrombosis 3–9×; no effect on restenosis
✕
"DES has more restenosis than BMS" → wrong; DES has LESS restenosis; DES has more late thrombosis
✕
"Aspirin + Coumadin post-DES" → wrong; anticoagulants ≠ antiplatelet; only add if separate indication

ICD Indications Post-STEMI

The most testable concept: do NOT implant ICD too early. LVEF must be reassessed after an adequate waiting period — the post-MI EF may recover significantly with revascularization and medical therapy.

The Waiting Rule — Most Important Concept

⏱ Wait Before Implanting ICD

Do NOT implant ICD based on early post-MI LVEF. Reassess at:

  • ≥ 40 days post-MI
  • ≥ 90 days post-revascularization

LVEF at 30 days does NOT predict final LVEF. No survival advantage to early ICD placement post-MI. While waiting: wearable cardioverter-defibrillator (WCD) is a Class IIb option.

ICD Thresholds — Primary Prevention

EFNYHA ClassICD Decision
≤ 35%II–IIIICD — Class I (most common scenario tested)
≤ 30%I (asymptomatic)ICD — Class I (even with no symptoms)
≤ 40% + NSVTAnyICD if inducible sustained VT on EP study (MADIT criteria) — Class IIa
≥ 45% and improvingAnyNo ICD — EF is preserved; does not meet threshold

VF Timing — Primary vs Secondary vs Late

Primary VF — First 48h

Acute ischaemia-mediated. No predisposing factors (no HF, shock, bundle branch block, aneurysm).

In-hospital mortality ↑, but long-term prognosis excellent after discharge. No ICD — treat the ischaemia.

Secondary VF — Due to Pump Failure

Occurs in setting of HF, shock, or extensive necrosis.

Poor prognosis.

Late VF — After 48h

Reflects a fixed arrhythmic substrate.

Increased in-hospital AND long-term mortality → consider ICD (secondary prevention).

⚠ Board Trap — Out-of-Hospital Arrest, EF 45% at 30 Days, Improving
Scenario: STEMI 30 days ago, cardiac arrest during acute phase, LVEF now 45% and improving — implant ICD?

Trap: "Patient had cardiac arrest — implant ICD now."

Why wrong: LVEF 45% does not meet the threshold (≤35% for NYHA II–III, ≤30% for NYHA I). An improving EF post-revascularization may normalise. ICD is not indicated when EF exceeds the threshold, regardless of the arrest history during the acute MI phase.

Correct: No ICD — EF is preserved and improving.

Board keyword: ICD threshold = EF, not arrest history; improving EF = wait and reassess
⚠ Board Trap — VF on Day 4 = ICD Indication?
Scenario: VF occurring 4 days after STEMI admission — implant ICD?

Trap: "VF = ICD indication."

Why wrong: VF in the first 48h = primary electrical complication of ischaemia = good long-term prognosis once ischaemia treated. Day 4 is after 48h → this is late VF → increased mortality → ICD should be considered (secondary prevention), not ruled out. But if VF was on day 1, no ICD.

Correct: VF first 48h → no ICD (primary electrical). VF after 48h → consider ICD.

Board keyword: 48h cutoff — before = primary = no ICD; after = secondary = consider ICD
❌ What to Eliminate — ICD Questions
✕
"LVEF 30% at day 10 → implant ICD immediately" → wrong; wait ≥40 days post-MI; EF may improve
✕
"EF 45% and improving → implant ICD (cardiac arrest history)" → wrong; EF above threshold = no ICD; arrest during acute phase is primary electrical
✕
"VF on day 1 → ICD" → wrong; VF in first 48h = primary electrical = no ICD; treat ischaemia

Complications

Mechanical Complications — The Surgical Three

Peak incidence: within 24 hours AND 3–5 days after STEMI (bimodal; range 1–14 days). Classic board pattern.

ComplicationMurmurKey Distinguishing Feature
Ventricular Septal Rupture (VSR)Harsh holosystolic + palpable thrillRight heart cath: O₂ step-up RA→RV (L-to-R shunt); biventricular failure
Free Wall RuptureNo specific murmur — often electromechanical dissociationSigns of tamponade: JVD, pulsus paradoxus, pericardial effusion; sudden death
Papillary Muscle RuptureSoft murmur, NO thrillNo O₂ step-up RA→RV; very high PCWP; severe pulmonary oedema; posteromedial > anterolateral PM

RV Infarction

Classic Presentation
  • Occurs in ~1/3 of inferior STEMI patients
  • Classic triad: JVD + clear lungs + hypotension (no pulmonary oedema)
  • Kussmaul's sign and hepatomegaly may be present
  • ECG: ST elevation in right-sided leads, especially V₄R (present in first 24h)
⚠ Management — What to Avoid
  • Treatment: Volume expansion to maintain RV preload
  • AVOID nitrates → venodilation → ↓ preload → cardiovascular collapse
  • AVOID diuretics → same mechanism; RV preload-dependent
  • If AV pacing needed: may require AV sequential (not just ventricular) pacing — RV infarct patients need atrial contribution

Killip Classification

ClassClinical SignsHistorical Mortality
INo signs of HF0–5%
IIRales at bases, S₃, tachypnea10–20%
IIIPulmonary oedema — rales >50% lung fields35–45%
IVCardiogenic shock — SBP <90, oliguria, cyanosis85–95%

Other Key Complications

Pericarditis Post-MI
  • Friction rub in transmural STEMI
  • Trapezius radiation = pericarditis, NOT STEMI recurrence
  • Treat with aspirin 650 mg 4× daily
  • Avoid anticoagulants — risk of haemorrhagic tamponade
  • Avoid NSAIDs/glucocorticoids — impair infarct healing, ↑ rupture risk
LV Aneurysm vs Pseudoaneurysm
  • True aneurysm: dyskinesis, scar tissue, does NOT rupture. Complications: HF, thrombus, arrhythmia
  • Pseudoaneurysm: myocardial rupture contained by pericardium; narrow neck; often ruptures → surgical repair if recognized
  • LV thrombus (esp. anterior MI) → ≥3 months oral anticoagulation
⚠ Board Trap — VSR vs Papillary Muscle Rupture
Scenario: Day 4 post-inferior STEMI, sudden pulmonary oedema, holosystolic murmur. Right heart catheterization findings?

Both VSR and PMR cause post-MI holosystolic murmur.

VSR: Loud murmur + palpable thrill + O₂ step-up RA→RV (left-to-right shunt) + biventricular failure.

PMR: Soft murmur + NO thrill + NO O₂ step-up + very high PCWP + severe pulmonary oedema only.

Key: Right heart cath O₂ step-up = VSR. No step-up + high PCWP = PMR.

Board keyword: VSR = thrill + O₂ step-up; PMR = no thrill + no step-up + high PCWP

Return to Activity & Secondary Prevention

Post-STEMI Activity Timeline

TimeframeActivity

After 24 hoursSit up, dangle legs, sit in chair
Hospitalisation (uncomplicated)3–5 days
First 1–2 weeks at homeWalking, light activity
Sexual activityAfter ~1 week (uncomplicated STEMI + PCI)
Return to work2–4 weeks (most patients)
After 2 weeksRegulate by exercise tolerance; cardiac rehabilitation

Long-Term Drug Therapy — Secondary Prevention

All Patients Post-STEMI
  • Aspirin indefinitely — 25% risk reduction in recurrent MI/stroke/CV death
  • DAPT (aspirin + P2Y₁₂) for 12 months
  • Beta blocker ≥1 year (continue indefinitely in most)
  • Statin — high-intensity (e.g., atorvastatin 40–80 mg)
If LVEF ≤40% or Clinical HF
  • ACE inhibitor (or ARB if intolerant) — reduces remodeling; continue indefinitely
  • Mineralocorticoid antagonist (eplerenone/spironolactone) if LVEF ≤40% + HF or DM
  • Avoid if: K⁺ ≥5.0 mEq/L or creatinine ≥2.5 mg/dL (men) / ≥2.0 mg/dL (women)
Electrolyte Targets Post-STEMI

K⁺ goal: ~4.5 mmol/L — hypokalemia increases VF risk

Mg goal: ~2.0 mmol/L — measure on admission; correct all deficits

⚠ Board Trap — Sexual Activity Post-STEMI
Q: When can sexual activity resume after uncomplicated STEMI + PCI?

Trap: "After 2 months" or "after 1 month."

Why wrong: Sexual activity demands ~3–5 METs — equivalent to climbing two flights of stairs. For uncomplicated STEMI, sexual activity can resume after approximately 1 week. 2 months is the restriction for major cardiac surgery or severe HF, not uncomplicated STEMI.

Correct: ~1 week post uncomplicated STEMI + PCI.

Board keyword: Uncomplicated STEMI = sexual activity after ~1 week (not 1–2 months)

Exam Traps — Board Exam Coach

All tested traps from the question bank, written as board-coach vignettes. Each includes: the scenario, the seductive wrong answer, the correct answer, the mechanism, and the board keyword.

⚠ Trap 01 — Women and the Lower ST Elevation Threshold
58F, 45 minutes of crushing substernal pressure, diaphoresis. ECG: 1.3 mm ST elevation in V₂, 1.4 mm in V₃. The ER physician says ECG does not meet STEMI criteria.

Trap: Applying the male 2 mm threshold to women in V₂–V₃.

Why wrong: Women require only ≥1.5 mm in V₂–V₃. This patient at 1.3–1.4 mm is close to but not quite the threshold — however, a clinician applying the male 2 mm threshold would incorrectly exclude her entirely. Women are systematically underdiagnosed this way.

Correct: Women = ≥1.5 mm (0.15 mV) in V₂–V₃. Men ≥40 = 2 mm. Men <40 = 2.5 mm.

Board keyword: Women V₂–V₃ threshold = 1.5 mm, not 2 mm
⚠ Trap 02 — Trapezius Radiation: STEMI or Pericarditis?
35M, chest pain radiating to both trapezius muscles, pleuritic quality, improves leaning forward. ECG: diffuse ST elevation, PR depression.

Trap: "ST elevation → STEMI → activate cath lab."

Why wrong: Trapezius radiation does NOT occur in STEMI — it is a specific distinguishing feature of pericarditis. Combined with diffuse (not regional) ST elevation, PR depression, pleuritic quality, and positional relief → pericarditis. If anticoagulants are given for presumed STEMI → tamponade risk.

Correct: Pericarditis. Treat with aspirin 650 mg 4× daily. No anticoagulants.

Board keyword: Trapezius radiation = pericarditis, not STEMI
⚠ Trap 03 — Nitrates + RV Infarction
67M, inferior STEMI (ST elevation II, III, aVF). BP 82/50, elevated JVP, clear lung fields. Nurse is about to give sublingual nitroglycerin for chest pain.

Trap: "Give nitrates — standard for STEMI chest pain."

Why wrong: Inferior STEMI + elevated JVP + clear lungs + hypotension = RV infarction. RV output entirely depends on preload. Nitrates → venodilation → ↓ preload → precipitous cardiovascular collapse. Same applies to diuretics.

Correct: Do NOT give nitrates. Treat with IV fluids to maintain RV preload.

Board keyword: Inferior MI + JVD + clear lungs + hypotension = RV infarct = no nitrates
⚠ Trap 04 — Prophylactic Lidocaine for VPBs
70M, STEMI admitted to cardiac unit. Frequent VPBs on monitor. Resident wants to start prophylactic IV lidocaine to prevent VF.

Trap: "Frequent VPBs in STEMI = antiarrhythmic therapy indicated."

Why wrong: Infrequent and even frequent VPBs occur in almost all STEMI patients and do not require treatment. Prophylactic antiarrhythmic therapy (lidocaine or oral agents) for VPBs may increase mortality rate. Multiple trials confirmed this. Beta blockers are the correct agents for VF prevention.

Correct: No antiarrhythmic. Give beta blockers. Prophylactic lidocaine = contraindicated.

Board keyword: VPBs in STEMI = beta blocker; prophylactic lidocaine = contraindicated = ↑mortality
⚠ Trap 05 — Nonculprit PCI in Cardiogenic Shock
65M, anterior STEMI + cardiogenic shock. Culprit LAD stented. High-grade RCA lesion also found. Cardiologist proposes to stent RCA during same procedure.

Trap: "Complete revascularization in STEMI is always better."

Why wrong: This is correct in STEMI without shock (PRAMI, CvLPRIT, COMPLETE trials). But in cardiogenic shock, routine nonculprit PCI during index procedure is contraindicated — associated with increased death or severe renal failure.

Correct: In shock → culprit-only PCI. Without shock → complete revascularization (culprit now, nonculprit within 45 days).

Board keyword: Shock = culprit-only; no shock = complete revascularization
⚠ Trap 06 — Primary VF vs Secondary VF: Opposite Prognosis
55M develops VF 6 hours after STEMI. No HF, no shock, no bundle branch block. Successfully defibrillated. Long-term prognosis?

Trap: "VF after STEMI = bad long-term prognosis; needs ICD."

Why wrong: This is primary VF — first 48h, no predisposing factors (HF, shock, BBB, aneurysm). Despite increased in-hospital mortality, long-term survival after hospital discharge is excellent. No ICD based on primary VF alone.

Correct: Primary VF (first 48h, no predisposing factors) = excellent long-term prognosis.

Board keyword: Primary VF = first 48h = excellent long-term prognosis; Secondary VF (pump failure) = poor
⚠ Trap 07 — DAPT Prevents Restenosis (Wrong Mechanism)
Patient post-DES asks: "If I stop my clopidogrel early, will my stent narrow again?" / Q: What is the consequence of stopping DAPT early after DES?

Trap: "Stopping DAPT raises restenosis risk."

Why wrong: DAPT prevents platelet-mediated thrombosis, not restenosis. Restenosis = neointimal hyperplasia (smooth muscle proliferation) — prevented by the DES drug coating. Stopping DAPT raises thrombosis risk 3–9×, with catastrophic consequences (death 10–20%, MI 30–70%). It has no effect on restenosis rate.

Correct: Stopping DAPT raises thrombosis risk 3–9×. Not restenosis risk.

Board keyword: Stopping DAPT = ↑thrombosis (catastrophic), NOT ↑restenosis
⚠ Trap 08 — ICD Too Early: LVEF 30% at 20 Days
60M, large anterior STEMI 20 days ago. Post-PCI LVEF 30%. NYHA Class II. Cardiologist proposes ICD implantation now.

Trap: "LVEF 30% + NYHA II = ICD Class I indication — implant now."

Why wrong: LVEF must be reassessed at ≥40 days post-MI and/or ≥90 days post-revascularization. No survival advantage to early ICD. LVEF may improve substantially with medical therapy and revascularization. While waiting: WCD is a Class IIb option.

Correct: Do not implant. Reassess LVEF at ≥40 days. Consider WCD in the interim.

Board keyword: Wait ≥40 days post-MI and ≥90 days post-revascularization before ICD implant
⚠ Trap 09 — NSAIDs for Post-MI Pericarditis
68M, Day 3 post-STEMI with pericarditic chest pain and friction rub. NP suggests adding ibuprofen for the pericarditis.

Trap: "Ibuprofen is the drug of choice for pericarditis."

Why wrong: NSAIDs and glucocorticoids impair infarct healing, increase risk of myocardial rupture, may result in a larger infarct scar, and increase coronary vascular resistance. They are contraindicated in STEMI. The only exception: aspirin — the correct treatment for post-MI pericarditis is aspirin 650 mg 4× daily.

Correct: Aspirin 650 mg 4× daily. No NSAIDs. No glucocorticoids.

Board keyword: Post-MI pericarditis = aspirin only; NSAIDs/steroids = contraindicated (impair healing)
⚠ Trap 10 — AIVR After Fibrinolysis: Benign Reperfusion Rhythm
59M receives tenecteplase for STEMI. Shortly after: wide complex rhythm at 75 bpm, regular, haemodynamically stable, asymptomatic.

Trap: "Wide complex = VT → give amiodarone."

Why wrong: Rate 60–100 bpm = accelerated idioventricular rhythm (AIVR), not VT. AIVR frequently occurs transiently during fibrinolysis at the moment of reperfusion — it is a reperfusion arrhythmia. Benign. Does not presage VT. Treat with monitoring only. Overly aggressive treatment can cause complete heart block.

Correct: AIVR — benign reperfusion rhythm. Monitor only. No antiarrhythmic needed.

Board keyword: AIVR (60–100 bpm) after fibrinolysis = reperfusion = benign = monitor only
