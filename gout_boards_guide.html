01

Overview & Crystal Types

Crystal-induced arthropathies arise when microcrystals deposit in joints and connective tissue, triggering acute or chronic inflammatory responses. Four major crystal types are responsible for the majority of crystal arthropathies:

Monosodium Urate (MSU)
Needle-shaped. Strongly negatively birefringent under compensated polarized light. Responsible for gout. Seen intracellularly + extracellularly.
Calcium Pyrophosphate (CPP)
Rectangular, rod-shaped, rhomboid. Weakly positively birefringent or nonbirefringent. Causes pseudogout (CPPD).
Calcium Apatite
Tiny (1–20 µm). Only seen by electron microscopy. Nonbirefringent globules. Stain red with alizarin red S. Causes Milwaukee shoulder and calcific tendinitis.
Calcium Oxalate (CaOx)
Bipyramidal shape. Strongly birefringent. Seen in chronic renal failure / dialysis patients. Stains with alizarin red S.
🔑 Board Pearl — Polarized Light Rule
Polarized light microscopy identifies MSU (negative birefringence) and CPP (weak positive birefringence). Apatite crystals are NOT visible by polarized light — they require electron microscopy. Aspiration is always indicated to rule out concurrent septic arthritis.
02

Pathogenesis of Gout

Why Humans Get Gout — Evolutionary Context

Humans are the only mammals known to develop gout spontaneously. This occurs because of a species-wide evolutionary loss of uricase, the enzyme that converts urate into the highly water-soluble allantoin. Without uricase, uric acid accumulates at physiologic pH (pKa 5.8) as urate (the ionized form), predisposing to crystal formation when serum concentrations exceed the saturation threshold of ~6.85 mg/dL.

Urate Balance: Production vs. Excretion

Serum urate reflects the balance between dietary intake, endogenous synthesis, and excretion:

Overproduction (10%)
  • Hematologic malignancies, psoriasis (↑ cell turnover → ↑ purine salvage)
  • Chemotherapy (↑ urate synthesis)
  • Tissue hypoxia (net ATP degradation → ADP → AMP → uric acid)
  • Glycogen storage diseases (types I, III, V, VIII)
  • Fructose intolerance/infusion
  • Ethanol
  • PRPP synthetase superactivity (X-linked) → ↑ PRPP → ↑ purines → ↑ urate
  • HPRT deficiency (Lesch-Nyhan) → hypoxanthine cannot be salvaged → converted to urate
VS
Impaired Excretion (90%)
  • Most common cause of hyperuricemia
  • Renal proximal tubule: URAT1 + OAT10 (apical urate reabsorption — sodium-driven)
  • GLUT9 transporter (basolateral excretion)
  • ABCG2 gene — high-capacity intestinal urate efflux transporter (also in renal proximal tubule)
  • Gut bacteria responsible for intestinal uricolysis
  • ~1/3 of daily uric acid elimination via GI tract; ~2/3 via renal
  • Drugs: diuretics (thiazide/loop), low-dose aspirin, pyrazinoate (PZA), nicotinate → anti-uricosuric

MSU Crystallization Cascade

1
Chronic Hyperuricemia (SU >~6.85 mg/dL)
Caused by obesity, Western diet, alcohol, diuretics, renal disease, genetics. Prerequisite for gout — but not sufficient alone; only a minority of hyperuricemic individuals develop gout.
2
Pathophysiologic Factors Favor Crystallization
Temperature (cooler peripheral joints), ↓ pH, intra-articular dehydration, ↑ cation concentration, collagen/chondroitin sulfate, nonaggregating proteoglycans.
3
MSU Crystal Deposition → Tophi Formation
Crystals deposit in joints and connective tissue. Synovial tophi typically walled off. Tophi found in proteoglycan-rich tissues (joints, tendons). Engage both innate and adaptive immunity.
4
Triggers → Crystal Shedding → Acute Flare
Triggers (purine-rich food, alcohol, diuresis, rapid urate changes with ULT initiation, local trauma, medical illness) loosen crystals from organic matrix → crystal shedding → interact with synovial cell lining and residential inflammatory cells.
5
NLRP3 Inflammasome Activation
Resident synovial phagocytes (monocytes/macrophages, mast cells) activated → NLRP3 inflammasome → pro-IL-1 and IL-1β, plus IL-6, IL-8, TNF-α. Neutrophils recruited → phagocytose MSU → respiratory burst → lysosomal enzymes, superoxide, leukotriene B4, more cytokines.

Genetic Causes of Hyperuricemia (High-Yield)

PRPP Synthetase Superactivity (X-linked)
  • ↑ PRPP → ↑ purine nucleotides → ↑ urate
  • Causes gout and urate nephrolithiasis
  • X chromosome-linked inborn error of metabolism
HPRT Deficiency (X-linked)
  • Spectrum: isolated hyperuricemia → Lesch-Nyhan syndrome (severe neurologic + behavioral abnormalities)
  • Without HPRT: hypoxanthine → only converted to urate (cannot be salvaged)
  • ↓ IMP and GMP → feedback inhibition lost → ↑ de novo purine synthesis
  • 250 different mutations described
  • Males homozygous → disease phenotype; Carrier females (esp. postmenopausal) → gout + urinary tract stones
  • Hyperuricemia in prepubertal boys → always consider HPRT/PRPP defects

Comorbidities of Gout (Cardiometabolic-Kidney Cluster)

⚠ Insulin Resistance Syndrome Link
Gout is often considered part of the insulin resistance syndrome. Associated comorbidities include: obesity, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, myocardial infarction, stroke, chronic kidney disease (CKD), and urate nephrolithiasis. Modifiable risk factors: obesity, Western diet, alcohol, sedentary lifestyle, diuretics.

Clinical Manifestations of Gout

Classic Presentation — Acute Gout Flare

Who Gets It
Middle-aged to elderly men; postmenopausal women. Women = only 5–20% of all gout patients, typically postmenopausal with OA, hypertension, or mild renal insufficiency, often on diuretics.
Timing
Onset at night to early morning. One of the most painful conditions humans experience. Subsides spontaneously in 1–2 weeks.
Joint Involvement Pattern
First MTP (podagra): 70–90% of cases. Then tarsal joints, ankles, knees. Fingers, wrists, elbows more common in elderly/advanced disease. Oligo- and polyarticular patterns develop over time.
Physical Exam Appearance
Warm, red, tender, substantially swollen joint and periarticular area. Mimics cellulitis (pseudocellulitis) — classic board trap.

Disease Stages

1
Asymptomatic Hyperuricemia
Elevated serum urate without clinical manifestations. Only a minority progress to gout.
2
Acute Recurrent Gout Flares
Episodic inflammatory attacks. Between attacks: intercritical gout — asymptomatic intervals of varying length, no residual symptoms.
3
Chronic Gouty Arthritis
After years of untreated flares: ongoing synovitis, subcutaneous tophi, deformity, bony destruction. Less commonly, chronic arthritis or tophi only as sole manifestation.

Flare Triggers (Critical for Boards)

  • Purine-rich food
  • Alcohol (especially beer)
  • Diuretic use (thiazide and loop)
  • Initiation of urate-lowering therapy — paradoxical flare from crystal shedding during rapid SU reduction
  • Local trauma
  • Medical illnesses: congestive heart failure, respiratory hypoxic conditions

Gout in Women — Special Considerations

  • Only 5–20% of all gout patients
  • Most are postmenopausal and elderly
  • Often have osteoarthritis, hypertension, or mild renal insufficiency
  • Usually receiving diuretics
  • Premenopausal gout is rare — if present, suspect inherited enzymatic deficit (HPRT/PRPP) or decreased renal urate clearance
🔬 Tophi — What Are They?
Tophi are deposits of MSU crystals surrounded by granulomatous inflammation, found in proteoglycan-rich tissues (joints, tendons). They represent chronic inflammation and engage both innate and adaptive immunity. Although they occasionally show acute inflammatory signs, tophi predominantly signify a chronic inflammatory state.
04

Diagnosis of Gout

Gold Standard: Synovial Fluid Analysis

Even when clinical features strongly suggest gout, needle aspiration and synovial fluid analysis is the gold standard to confirm the diagnosis. This is critical because: acute septic arthritis, other crystal arthropathies, palindromic rheumatism, and psoriatic arthritis can all mimic gout.

MSU Crystals on Polarized Light
  • Needle-shaped and rod-shaped
  • Strongly negatively birefringent under compensated polarized light
  • Seen both intracellularly and extracellularly
  • Leukocytes: 5,000–75,000/µL (cloudy fluid)
  • Large crystal loads → thick, pasty, chalky fluid
Important Caveats
  • Always culture the fluid — bacterial infection can coexist with MSU crystals
  • MSU crystals can be demonstrated between flares in first MTP and asymptomatic knee → useful for diagnosis during intercritical period
  • Aspiration of asymptomatic joints is diagnostically useful

Serum Urate — Tricky Interpretation

⚠ Board Trap — Serum Urate During Flare
Serum urate can be normal or even LOW during an acute gout flare. Inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-6) have uricosuric properties that can lower serum urate by ~2 mg/dL. Do not use serum urate to rule out gout during an acute attack! Instead, seek historical or post-flare serum urate values.

Serum urate is almost always elevated at some point in a gout patient's lifetime — use historical values as a diagnostic clue and ULT therapeutic target.

Baseline Labs to Order

  • Serum creatinine (renal function — affects drug dosing)
  • Liver function tests (for allopurinol monitoring)
  • Hemoglobin + WBC count
  • Hemoglobin A1c (diabetes comorbidity)
  • Serum lipids (cardiovascular risk)

Imaging

Musculoskeletal Ultrasound
Reveals double-contour sign overlying articular cartilage — signifies MSU deposition. Useful for early diagnosis.
Dual-Energy CT (DECT)
Uses two different energy beams to identify MSU crystals by chemical composition. Can confirm specific presence of MSU crystals.
Plain Radiography
Findings seen in advanced/chronic gout only:
  • Cystic changes
  • Well-defined erosions with sclerotic margins + overhanging bony edges
  • Soft tissue masses (tophi)
Typically absent in early gout!

Acute Gout Treatment

🔑 Principle of Acute Management
The goal is anti-inflammatory therapy targeting the downstream process of crystal-induced inflammation. Nonpharmacologic measures (ice packs, rest of involved joints) are helpful adjuncts. Choice of agent depends on comorbidities, concurrent medications, and prior response.

Early Initiation Is Critical

For established gout patients with recurrent flares, patients should be given a supply of medications ready to start at the first sign of a flare. Early initiation helps abort or reduce flare severity.

Colchicine
Anti-inflammatory · Microtubule Inhibitor
Mechanism Inhibits microtubule polymerization → disrupts neutrophil chemotaxis and activation; also inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome
Dose — Acute (Low-Dose Regimen) 1.2 mg at first sign of flare, then 0.6 mg 1 hour later; subsequent-day dosing per response. As effective as high-dose but better tolerated.
Prophylactic Dose 0.6 mg once to twice daily (during ULT initiation, continue for 3–6 months until normouricemic + attack-free, or until tophi disappear)
Metabolism/Elimination P-glycoprotein (MDR1) in liver, small intestine, kidneys; also CYP3A4 (hepatic)
Drug Interactions Reduce dose with P-glycoprotein or CYP3A4 inhibitors: clarithromycin, tacrolimus. Extra caution with hepatorenal impairment.
Dose Adjustment Reduce in renal disease. Lower doses for prolonged use in CKD.
💊 Pearl
Colchicine works best when used early in a flare. If delayed, efficacy declines. The low-dose regimen (1.2/0.6 mg) has equivalent efficacy to high-dose with fewer GI side effects.
NSAIDs
Anti-inflammatory · COX Inhibitors
Best For Individuals without complicating comorbidities. Most commonly used class for acute gout.
Effective Agents & Doses
  • Indomethacin: 25–50 mg TID
  • Naproxen: 500 mg BID
  • Ibuprofen: 800 mg TID
  • Celecoxib: 800 mg × 1, then 400 mg 12h later, then 400 mg BID
Key Considerations Give in full anti-inflammatory doses. Effective in vast majority of patients. Avoid in renal insufficiency, GI disease, active peptic ulcer, heart failure.
Glucocorticoids
Anti-inflammatory · Steroid
Oral Prednisone 30–50 mg/day initial, then gradually tapered with resolution. Effective even in polyarticular gout.
Intramuscular IM injection effective for polyarticular gout when oral route not feasible.
Intraarticular Effective and well tolerated for single joint or few involved joints. Preferred when other routes contraindicated.
Use Case When NSAIDs and colchicine are contraindicated (renal disease, GI disease, drug interactions).
Anakinra / Canakinumab
IL-1β Antagonist · Biologic
Mechanism Block IL-1β — the central proinflammatory cytokine activated by the NLRP3 inflammasome in gout
Indication When other treatments are contraindicated or have failed. Useful in patients who cannot tolerate NSAIDs, colchicine, or glucocorticoids.

Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT)

🎯 Goal of ULT
Correct the underlying chronic hyperuricemia. Target serum urate <5–6 mg/dL (<300–360 µmol/L) — below the MSU saturation point — to prevent recurrent flares and eliminate tophi. Usually required for life.

When to Initiate ULT

  • Consider when hyperuricemia cannot be corrected by lifestyle changes alone
  • Indications: ≥2 gout flares per year; severe/prolonged flares; subcutaneous tophi; chronic gouty arthritis; known uric acid nephrolithiasis
  • Patient willingness to commit to long-term therapy
⚠ Timing Rule
ULT is generally NOT initiated during active acute gout flares — acutely lowering SU can worsen the flare (crystal shedding). Start during the resolution phase or after a flare, with anti-inflammatory prophylaxis (colchicine 0.6 mg 1–2×/day or naproxen 250 mg BID).

Paradoxical flares during ULT initiation = inflammatory reactions to MSU crystals shed from dissolving synovial tophi during rapid SU reduction. Faster urate reduction → higher flare risk.

Lifestyle Modifications (First-Line Non-Drug)

  • Control body weight
  • Healthy diet (↓ purine-rich foods)
  • Limit alcohol use
  • Decrease fructose-rich foods and beverages
  • Avoid thiazide and loop diuretics when possible
Allopurinol
First-Line ULT · Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor
Mechanism Inhibits xanthine oxidase → ↓ urate synthesis (xanthine cannot be converted to urate)
Dosing Strategy Start 100 mg/day (or less) once daily; titrate up to 800 mg/day max to achieve target SU <5–6 mg/dL
CKD Dosing GFR <30 mL/min → start at 50 mg/day, titrate up slowly. Start low, go slow → reduces SCAR risk.
Side Effects Mild cutaneous reactions in ~2% of users. Rarely: Severe Cutaneous Adverse Reactions (SCARs) including Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis — rare but life-threatening.
SCAR Risk Factors CKD, higher initial dosing (>100 mg/day in CKD), HLA-B*5801 carriage
HLA-B*5801 Screening Required before starting allopurinol in: Southeast Asian (except Japanese), Pacific Islander/Native Hawaiian, and Black patients — due to high carriage rate in these populations → racial disparity in SCAR risk. If positive → use alternative agent.
🚨 Critical Safety Pearl
HLA-B*5801 carriage is the major genetic risk factor for allopurinol-induced SCARs. Screen Southeast Asian, Pacific Islander/Native Hawaiian, and Black patients before prescribing. If carrier → switch to febuxostat.
Febuxostat
Second Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor
Mechanism Xanthine oxidase inhibitor (different structure from allopurinol)
Metabolism Predominantly via glucuronide formation and oxidation in liver
Advantage over Allopurinol Does NOT require dose adjustment in moderate-to-severe CKD. Used when allopurinol not tolerated or in HLA-B*5801 carriers.
Probenecid / Uricosuric Agents
Second-Line ULT · Uricosuric
Mechanism Inhibits renal tubular reabsorption of urate (URAT1/OAT10) → ↑ urinary urate excretion
Probenecid Dose Start 250 mg BID → increase gradually up to 3 g/day
Limitations NOT effective when serum creatinine >177 µmol/L (2 mg/dL). Requires adequate renal function.
Benzbromarone Another uricosuric (not available in the US) — more effective in CKD patients.
Use Second-line, alone or in combination with xanthine oxidase inhibitors. For patients with good renal function.
Pegloticase
Pegylated Uricase · Recombinant Enzyme
Mechanism Converts urate to allantoin (highly water-soluble, easily excreted) — replaces the uricase activity lost in human evolution
Indication Patients who do NOT tolerate or FAIL full doses of other ULT agents
Immunogenicity Issue Often used in combination with methotrexate to reduce immunogenicity (antibody formation leads to loss of efficacy and infusion reactions)

Cardiometabolic Drugs with Urate-Lowering Effects

Therapies for gout comorbidities that also lower serum urate:

SGLT2 Inhibitors
Offer both urate reduction AND comprehensive cardiometabolic-kidney comorbidity care. Highly beneficial in gout patients with multiple comorbidities.
Other Agents
Losartan (ARB), amlodipine (CCB), fenofibrate (lipid-lowering) — all have modest urate-lowering effects as a secondary benefit.

Calcium Pyrophosphate Deposition (CPPD)

Epidemiology & Pathogenesis

Epidemiology
  • Predominantly affects the elderly
  • Prevalence doubles with each decade after 60 years
  • ~50% prevalence in those >85 years old
  • Most cases asymptomatic
Central Pathogenic Mechanism
  • Increased inorganic pyrophosphate (PPi) in cartilage matrix — analogous to hyperuricemia in gout
  • PPi combines with calcium → CPP crystals in matrix vesicles or on collagen fibers
  • Most PPi derives from breakdown of extracellular ATP
  • ANKH protein (multipass membrane protein) regulates ATP efflux and PPi transport
  • ANKH gene mutations → ↑ PPi elaboration → familial and sporadic CPPD
  • ↑ ATP pyrophosphohydrolase and 5'-nucleotidase activity also ↑ PPi

Associated Conditions (CPPD in Age <50 → Investigate!)

A minority of CPPD patients have metabolic abnormalities. CPPD in patients <50 years old warrants investigation for:

Endocrine-Metabolic Causes
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism (↑ calcium)
  • Hemochromatosis (↑ iron)
  • Hypophosphatasia (↓ alkaline phosphatase)
  • Hypomagnesemia (↓ magnesium)
  • Gitelman's syndrome
  • X-linked hypophosphatemic rickets
  • Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia
Other Associations
  • Aging (strongest association)
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Post-meniscectomy or joint trauma
  • Familial-genetic (ANKH mutations)
  • Certain drugs: thiazide and loop diuretics, possibly proton pump inhibitors
  • Malabsorption
  • Gout (crystals can coexist)
🔑 Workup for Young CPPD Patients
Serum: calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, magnesium, iron, and transferrin. Plus inquiry for familial aggregation.

Clinical Manifestations

Acute CPP Crystal Arthritis ("Pseudogout")
  • Knee most commonly affected (not first MTP like gout)
  • Wrist second most common
  • Other: shoulder, ankle, elbow, hands
  • First MTP (podagra) rarely affected — key distinction from gout
  • Initial episodes last longer than gout: up to weeks to months (vs 1–2 weeks for gout)
  • May have systemic signs: fever, chills, elevated acute-phase reactants
  • Up to 50% of attacks associated with low-grade fever; occasionally 40°C (104°F)
Triggers for Acute CPPD
  • Trauma
  • Severe medical illness
  • Surgery — especially parathyroidectomy (rapid ↓ calcium and magnesium → crystal dissolution and shedding)

Chronic CPPD Patterns

  • Pseudo-osteoarthritis (most common) — polyarticular arthritis resembling OA; affects atypical OA joints (metacarpophalangeal, wrist, elbow, shoulder, ankle)
  • Chronic symmetric synovitis resembling rheumatoid arthritis
  • Severe destructive disease resembling neuropathic arthritis
  • Intervertebral disk and ligament calcification with restricted spine mobility
  • Crowned dens syndrome
  • Spinal stenosis (commonly elderly)
  • Rarely: tumoral deposits of CPP in soft tissues

Diagnosis of CPPD

Synovial Fluid
Rhomboid or rod-like crystals. Weakly positively birefringent or nonbirefringent under polarized light. Leukocytes: several thousand to 100,000/µL (mean ~24,000/µL); predominant cell = neutrophil.
Chondrocalcinosis on Imaging
Punctate and/or linear radiodense deposits within fibrocartilaginous joint menisci or articular hyaline cartilage. Also visible on ultrasound. Presumptive of CPPD in absence of effusion.
Must Rule Out
Infection (synovial fluid cultures essential when fever present). CPP crystals can coexist with MSU and apatite — examination of fluid must distinguish all types. Exception: chondrocalcinosis due to CaOx in chronic renal failure.

Treatment of CPPD

🔑 Key Distinction from Gout Treatment
Unlike gout, there is no effective way to remove CPP crystal deposits from cartilage and synovium. There is no equivalent of urate-lowering therapy for CPPD.
  • Acute attacks: Ice packs + rest; joint fluid aspiration + glucocorticoid injection; oral colchicine; NSAIDs; systemic glucocorticoids
  • Severe polyarticular attacks: IL-1β antagonist anakinra
  • Prophylaxis (frequent recurrent attacks): Daily colchicine
  • Chronic synovitis: NSAIDs + gastric protection; colchicine; low-dose glucocorticoids; hydroxychloroquine; methotrexate
  • Progressive destructive large-joint arthropathy: May require joint replacement

Calcium Apatite Deposition Disease

Pathogenesis

Apatite is the primary mineral of normal bone and teeth. Abnormal accumulation of basic calcium phosphates (largely carbonate-substituted apatite) occurs in:

  • Areas of tissue damage (dystrophic calcification)
  • Hypercalcemic or hyperparathyroid states (metastatic calcification)
  • Connective tissue diseases (calcinosis — systemic sclerosis, dermatomyositis)
  • Chronic renal failure: hyperphosphatemia → extensive apatite deposition in and around joints (tumoral calcinosis, calciphylaxis)

30–50% of OA patients have apatite microcrystals in synovial fluid. Apatite crystals cause synovial lining cells to undergo mitosis and release PGE2, cytokines, collagenases, and neutral proteases → destructive potential.

Clinical Manifestations

Periarticular
  • Calcific periarthritis (e.g., supraspinatus tendon rupture)
  • Bursitis and tendinitis
  • Hydroxyapatite pseudopodagra
  • Polyarticular involvement
  • Asymptomatic deposition (most common)
Articular — Milwaukee Shoulder
Hemorrhagic shoulder effusions in elderly — apatite aggregates in synovial fluid. Also occurs in hips, knees, erosive OA of fingers. Joint destruction from damage to cartilage + supporting structures → instability, deformity. Symptoms: minimal to severe pain → may require joint replacement.

Diagnosis

  • Synovial fluid leukocyte count usually LOW (<2,000/µL) despite dramatic symptoms — with predominance of mononuclear cells
  • Individual crystals very small — only visible by electron microscopy
  • Clumps: 1–20 µm shiny nonbirefringent globules; stain purplish with Wright's stain, bright red with alizarin red S
  • Definitive identification: electron microscopy + energy-dispersive elemental analysis, x-ray diffraction, infrared spectroscopy, or Raman microspectroscopy (usually not required clinically)
  • Imaging: intra/periarticular calcifications; distinguish from linear calcifications of CPPD
⚠ Polarized Light Rule for Apatite
Apatite crystals are NONBIREFRINGENT — they cannot be identified by standard polarized light microscopy. This is a key distinction.

Treatment

  • Treatment is nonspecific
  • Acute bursitis/synovitis may be self-limiting (days to weeks)
  • NSAIDs or oral colchicine for 2 weeks, OR intra/periarticular depot glucocorticoid injection → shorten duration and intensity
  • Periarticular deposits may resorb with resolution of attacks
  • In renal failure on hemodialysis: agents to lower serum phosphate may lead to deposit resorption
  • Underlying severe destructive changes → poor response to medical therapy

Calcium Oxalate (CaOx) Deposition Disease

Pathogenesis

  • CaOx deposits recognized in visceral organs, blood vessels, bones, cartilage in chronic renal disease
  • Reported patients have been on long-term hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis
  • Many had received ascorbic acid supplements: ascorbic acid → metabolized to oxalate → inadequately cleared in uremia and by dialysis
  • CaOx aggregates in bone, articular cartilage, synovium, periarticular tissues → crystal shedding → acute synovitis
  • Like apatite and CPP: persistent CaOx aggregates → stimulate synovial cell proliferation + enzyme release → progressive articular destruction
  • Primary oxalosis: rare hereditary metabolic disorder → acute or chronic CaOx arthritis, periarthritis, bone disease
  • Avoid ascorbic acid supplements and oxalate-rich foods in dialysis patients

Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis

  • Clinical features may not be distinguishable from MSU, CPP, or apatite arthritis
  • Joints: fingers, wrists, elbows, knees, ankles, feet
  • Radiographs: chondrocalcinosis or soft tissue calcifications
  • Synovial fluid: usually noninflammatory (<2,000 leukocytes/µL) or mildly inflammatory
  • Crystal shape: bipyramidal (most easily recognized) — strongly birefringent under polarized light
  • Variable shape; variable birefringence; stain with alizarin red S

Treatment

  • NSAIDs, colchicine, intraarticular glucocorticoids, and/or increased dialysis frequency → only slight improvement
  • In primary oxalosis: liver transplantation → significant reduction in crystal deposits

High-Yield Distinctions

Gout Pseudocellulitis Trap
Acute gout flare produces warm, red, tender, swollen joint that closely mimics cellulitis. Distinguishing feature: joint involvement (arthritis), not skin infection. Synovial fluid aspiration confirms.
Urate During Acute Flare
Serum urate may be normal or low during an acute gout attack (IL-6 uricosuric effect). Never use a normal SU to exclude gout in an acute setting.
Gout vs. Pseudogout — Joint Location
Gout: first MTP (podagra) most common. Pseudogout: knee most common; first MTP rarely affected. Wrist involvement is more characteristic of CPPD.
ULT Timing Rule
Do NOT start ULT during an active flare (can worsen it via crystal shedding). Start with anti-inflammatory prophylaxis when initiating ULT. Faster SU reduction = higher flare risk.
HLA-B*5801 Alert
Screen Southeast Asian (non-Japanese), Pacific Islander/Native Hawaiian, and Black patients for HLA-B*5801 before prescribing allopurinol. Positive → use febuxostat instead.
Parathyroidectomy → CPPD Flare
Surgery (especially parathyroidectomy) → rapid ↓ calcium and magnesium → CPP crystal dissolution and shedding → acute pseudogout attack. Classic precipitant.

Exam Traps — Board Coach Mode

🎯 Expert Board Exam Coach
The following traps are based exclusively on material in this chapter. Each trap is designed to present the distractor exactly as it appears on boards — do not be fooled.
★ TRAP 1 — Look-Alike
A 52-year-old man presents with sudden onset of severe pain, redness, warmth, and swelling of his right first metatarsophalangeal joint that began last night. The skin appears erythematous and shiny, and the patient says it "looks infected." Serum uric acid is 5.8 mg/dL. Blood cultures are ordered. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Distractor Cellulitis or septic arthritis — the joint looks infected, and the serum urate is NORMAL, making gout seem less likely.
Correct Answer Acute gout flare (pseudocellulitis). The presentation of podagra with nocturnal onset, first MTP involvement, and dramatic local inflammation is classic for gout. A normal serum urate during an acute flare does NOT exclude gout — inflammatory cytokines (especially IL-6) have uricosuric properties that can lower SU by ~2 mg/dL. Diagnosis is confirmed by synovial fluid aspiration showing negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals.
Mechanism IL-6 lowers serum urate during acute inflammation. Do not use SU to rule out gout in the acute setting.
🔑 Board Keyword: "SU normal during flare"
★ TRAP 2 — Number Trap
A patient with established gout is started on allopurinol. What serum urate target should you aim for, and what is the saturation threshold for MSU crystal formation?
Distractor Confusing the two numbers: MSU saturation (~6.85 mg/dL) vs. therapeutic target (<6 mg/dL) vs. more aggressive target (<5 mg/dL in patients with tophi).
Correct Answer Target SU <5–6 mg/dL (300–360 µmol/L) — a sub-saturation point to dissolve existing crystals and prevent new ones. MSU saturation occurs at ~6.85 mg/dL. The therapeutic target is deliberately set BELOW saturation to drive crystal dissolution.
Mechanism Keeping SU below the saturation threshold allows existing tophi to gradually dissolve via reverse diffusion of urate from crystal deposits.
🔑 Board Keyword: "sub-saturation target"
★ TRAP 3 — Timing Trap
A patient with gout comes in with an active flare. The attending wants to start allopurinol immediately to address his hyperuricemia. What do you advise?
Distractor Starting allopurinol right away seems logical — treat the underlying cause while treating the flare simultaneously.
Correct Answer Do NOT initiate ULT during an active gout flare. Rapidly lowering serum urate causes paradoxical crystal shedding from dissolving synovial tophi — worsening the acute flare. Wait until the flare resolves, then start allopurinol with colchicine prophylaxis (0.6 mg 1–2×/day) to cover the paradoxical flare risk.
Mechanism Rapid SU reduction destabilizes existing MSU crystal deposits → crystal shedding → acute synovitis. Anti-inflammatory prophylaxis mitigates this.
🔑 Board Keyword: "never start ULT during active flare"
★ TRAP 4 — Population Trap (HLA)
A 58-year-old Korean man with gout is being considered for urate-lowering therapy. What must you do before prescribing allopurinol?
Distractor Just start allopurinol at a low dose — 100 mg/day is the standard starting dose, and allopurinol is first-line.
Correct Answer Screen for HLA-B*5801 before starting allopurinol. Korean patients (and other Southeast Asian populations, except Japanese descendants; Pacific Islander/Native Hawaiian; and Black patients) have a high carriage rate of HLA-B*5801, which dramatically increases risk of life-threatening severe cutaneous adverse reactions (SCARs), including Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis. If positive, use febuxostat instead.
Mechanism HLA-B*5801 creates immunologic susceptibility to allopurinol-induced hypersensitivity. The allele is distributed unevenly among ethnic groups, creating racial disparity in SCAR risk.
🔑 Board Keyword: "Southeast Asian + allopurinol → HLA-B*5801"
★ TRAP 5 — Look-Alike (Gout vs. Pseudogout)
An 80-year-old woman presents with acute, severe knee joint inflammation with fever of 38.9°C and elevated ESR/CRP. Synovial fluid shows 30,000 WBC/µL. Crystals are identified that are rectangular/rhomboid and weakly positively birefringent. What is the diagnosis, and how does it differ from gout?
Distractor The fever and elevated inflammatory markers suggest septic arthritis. The knee joint involvement looks unusual for gout.
Correct Answer Acute CPP crystal arthritis (pseudogout). The CPP crystal characteristics (rhomboid shape, weakly positive birefringence) distinguish it from MSU (needle-shaped, strongly negative birefringence). Up to 50% of CPPD attacks feature fever, occasionally up to 40°C. However, because infection can coexist with crystals, always culture the synovial fluid.
Mechanism CPP crystals activate the NLRP3 inflammasome → IL-1β → same acute inflammatory cascade as MSU. Fever is common in CPPD (less common in gout).
🔑 Board Keyword: "rhomboid + weakly positive birefringence = CPP"
★ TRAP 6 — Drug Interaction Trap
A gout patient on colchicine for acute flare is prescribed clarithromycin for a respiratory infection. What is the concern?
Distractor Colchicine and clarithromycin are commonly used drugs; the interaction is easily overlooked.
Correct Answer Clarithromycin is a CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein inhibitor → reduces colchicine clearance → colchicine toxicity. Colchicine is eliminated via P-glycoprotein (MDR1) in the liver/intestine/kidneys AND via CYP3A4. Inhibiting either pathway increases colchicine levels. The colchicine dose must be reduced, or the drug combination avoided — especially in patients with renal or hepatic impairment.
Mechanism Colchicine has a narrow therapeutic index. CYP3A4/P-gp inhibitors (clarithromycin, tacrolimus, cyclosporine) increase colchicine levels → toxicity (myopathy, cytopenias, GI toxicity).
🔑 Board Keyword: "colchicine + clarithromycin = CYP3A4/P-gp interaction"
★ TRAP 7 — Mechanism Trap (Which ULT Agent?)
A gout patient with a serum creatinine of 2.3 mg/dL is being considered for uricosuric therapy with probenecid. Is this appropriate?
Distractor Probenecid is a valid second-line ULT agent — it seems reasonable to try it.
Correct Answer No — probenecid is contraindicated/ineffective when serum creatinine >177 µmol/L (2 mg/dL). Uricosuric agents require adequate renal function to increase urate excretion. This patient's creatinine of 2.3 mg/dL exceeds this threshold. Consider febuxostat (xanthine oxidase inhibitor that does not require dose adjustment in moderate-to-severe CKD) or benzbromarone (if available).
Mechanism Probenecid works by blocking renal tubular urate reabsorption (URAT1/OAT10). Impaired renal function reduces the tubular substrate for drug action AND increases SCAR risk for allopurinol.
🔑 Board Keyword: "probenecid ineffective when Cr >2 mg/dL"
★ TRAP 8 — Population Trap (Young CPPD)
A 42-year-old man presents with acute inflammatory arthritis of the knee. Synovial fluid reveals rhomboid crystals with weak positive birefringence. What additional workup must be pursued, and why?
Distractor This is simply CPPD disease — treat symptomatically with colchicine/NSAIDs and move on.
Correct Answer CPPD in patients under 50 requires evaluation for underlying metabolic disorders. Order: serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, magnesium, iron, and transferrin. Inquire about familial aggregation. Evaluate for: primary hyperparathyroidism, hemochromatosis, hypophosphatasia, hypomagnesemia, Gitelman's syndrome, and hereditary CPPD (ANKH mutations). CPPD in this age group almost always has an underlying correctable cause.
Mechanism Metabolic products (↑ calcium, ↑ iron, ↓ alkaline phosphatase/pyrophosphatase) alter cartilage environment to favor CPP crystal nucleation. Treating the underlying condition may prevent progression.
🔑 Board Keyword: "CPPD under age 50 → metabolic workup"
★ TRAP 9 — Look-Alike (Lesch-Nyhan Trap)
A 12-year-old boy is brought in with gout-like symptoms and hyperuricemia. He also displays self-mutilating behavior and developmental delay. What is the underlying etiology?
Distractor Treat the hyperuricemia with allopurinol and investigate behavioral issues separately.
Correct Answer Lesch-Nyhan syndrome — HPRT deficiency. HPRT deficiency is X-linked and causes a spectrum from isolated hyperuricemia to full Lesch-Nyhan syndrome (severe neurologic + behavioral abnormalities including self-mutilation). Hyperuricemia in prepubertal boys should ALWAYS prompt evaluation for HPRT deficiency and PRPP synthetase superactivity. Males homozygous for the X-linked mutation manifest the disease.
Mechanism Without HPRT, hypoxanthine cannot be salvaged → exclusively degraded to urate. Also causes ↓ IMP and GMP → loss of feedback inhibition → ↑ de novo purine synthesis → more urate.
🔑 Board Keyword: "prepubertal boy + hyperuricemia + neurologic = HPRT deficiency"
★ TRAP 10 — Special Population (Dialysis + CaOx)
A 66-year-old patient on long-term hemodialysis presents with acute arthritis of the wrist. She has been taking vitamin C supplements for several months. Synovial fluid shows bipyramidal, strongly birefringent crystals. What is the diagnosis, and what dietary/supplement change is critical?
Distractor Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) supplements are generally benign and beneficial antioxidants — no action needed beyond joint treatment.
Correct Answer Calcium oxalate (CaOx) crystal arthritis. In renal failure/dialysis patients, ascorbic acid is metabolized to oxalate, which is inadequately cleared by uremia and dialysis. Oxalate accumulates → CaOx crystal deposition in joints. Ascorbic acid supplements and oxalate-rich foods must be AVOIDED in dialysis patients. Bipyramidal strongly birefringent crystals on synovial fluid analysis are diagnostic.
Mechanism Ascorbic acid → oxalate → hyperoxalosis in uremic patients. Inadequate dialysis clearance of oxalate → joint/periarticular crystal deposition.
🔑 Board Keyword: "dialysis + vitamin C + bipyramidal crystals = CaOx arthritis"
